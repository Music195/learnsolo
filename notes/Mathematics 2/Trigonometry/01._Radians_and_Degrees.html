Radians and Degrees
Radians and degrees are two different units for measuring angles. A radian is defined as the angle subtended by an arc equal in length to the radius, providing a more natural unit for mathematical calculations.
Definition:
1 radian = angle with arc length = radius
Unit circle: circumference = 2πr
Conversion:
180° = π radians
1° = π/180 radians
1 radian = 180°/π ≈ 57.3°
Full Circle:
360° = 2π radians
Fundamental relationship
The radian system is preferred in mathematics because it simplifies many trigonometric formulas and calculus operations. The unit circle naturally leads to radian measure.

Conversion Between Degrees and Radians

Degrees to Radians

$$\theta_{\text{radians}} = \theta_{\text{degrees}} \times \frac{\pi}{180}$$
Example: 90° = 90 × (π/180) = π/2 radians
30° = π/6 radians

Radians to Degrees

$$\theta_{\text{degrees}} = \theta_{\text{radians}} \times \frac{180}{\pi}$$
Example: π radians = π × (180/π) = 180°
π/2 radians = 90°

Common Conversions

\begin{aligned} 0° &= 0 \\ 30° &= \pi/6 \\ 45° &= \pi/4 \\ 60° &= \pi/3 \\ 90° &= \pi/2 \\ 180° &= \pi \\ 270° &= 3\pi/2 \\ 360° &= 2\pi \end{aligned}
Key angles: Multiples of 30° and 45°

Mathematical Foundation

The radian system is based on the geometry of circles and provides a natural unit for angle measurement:

  • Definition: One radian is the angle subtended at the center of a circle by an arc equal in length to the radius
  • Unit circle: A circle with radius 1 unit has circumference 2π, so one full rotation is 2π radians
  • Conversion factor: 180° = π radians, so the conversion factor is π/180 or 180/π
  • Arc length formula: s = rθ (in radians), where s is arc length, r is radius, θ is angle in radians
  • Area of sector: A = (1/2)r²θ (in radians), where A is sector area, r is radius, θ is angle in radians
  • Why radians? Many mathematical formulas become simpler when angles are measured in radians
  • Small angle approximation: For small angles, sinθ ≈ θ (in radians), cosθ ≈ 1 - θ²/2
  • Period of trig functions: sin(x) and cos(x) have period 2π when x is in radians
  • Calculus connection: Derivatives and integrals of trigonometric functions are simpler in radians
  • Applications: Physics, engineering, computer graphics, navigation systems
\begin{aligned} \text{Arc length:} \quad s &= r\theta \quad (\theta \text{ in radians}) \\ \text{Sector area:} \quad A &= \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta \quad (\theta \text{ in radians}) \\ \text{Conversion:} \quad \theta_{\text{rad}} &= \theta_{\text{deg}} \times \frac{\pi}{180} \\ \theta_{\text{deg}} &= \theta_{\text{rad}} \times \frac{180}{\pi} \\ \text{Full circle:} \quad 360° &= 2\pi \text{ radians} \end{aligned}

Practice Problems

Problem 1

Convert 120° to radians. Express your answer in terms of π.

Solution:

120° × (π/180) = (120π)/180 = (2π)/3

Result: 120° = 2π/3 radians

Problem 2

Convert 5π/4 radians to degrees.

Solution:

(5π/4) × (180/π) = (5/4) × 180 = 225°

Result: 5π/4 radians = 225°

Problem 3

Find the arc length on a circle of radius 10 cm subtended by a central angle of 2.5 radians.

Solution:

Arc length s = rθ = 10 × 2.5 = 25 cm

Result: Arc length = 25 cm

Key Takeaways
  • A radian is the angle subtended by an arc equal to the radius length
  • Full circle: 360° = 2π radians (fundamental conversion)
  • Degrees to radians: multiply by π/180
  • Radians to degrees: multiply by 180/π
  • Arc length formula s = rθ works only when θ is in radians
  • Sector area formula A = (1/2)r²θ works only when θ is in radians
  • Trigonometric functions and calculus are simpler in radians
  • Common angles: 30° = π/6, 45° = π/4, 60° = π/3, 90° = π/2
  • Unit circle has circumference 2π, so 2π radians = 360°
  • Radians are dimensionless (pure numbers), degrees are not
