Equation of a Line (Point-Slope Form)
The point-slope form uses a known point on the line and the slope to define the equation of a straight line in the coordinate plane.
Point-Slope Form: $$y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$$
$$\text{Where m is slope, (x₁,y₁) is a point on the line}$$
Slope Definition: $$m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$$
$$\text{Change in y over change in x}$$
Two-Point Form: $$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$
$$\text{Slope between two points}$$
The point-slope form is particularly useful when you know one point on the line and the slope. The slope m represents the steepness of the line - how much y changes for each unit change in x.
Different Forms of Line Equations

Point-Slope Form

y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)
$$y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$$
Uses: Known point and slope
Advantages: Easy to derive from given information
Applications: Tangent lines, rate of change problems

Slope-Intercept Form

y = mx + b
$$y = mx + b$$
Uses: Slope and y-intercept
Advantages: Shows y-intercept directly
Applications: Linear functions, graphing

Two-Point Form

Two points determine a line
$$\frac{y - y_1}{x - x_1} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$
Uses: Two points on the line
Advantages: No need to calculate slope first
Applications: Connecting two known points

Mathematical Foundation

The point-slope form is derived from the slope concept and coordinate geometry:

  • Slope Formula: m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁) represents the rate of change
  • Linear Relationship: For any two points on a line, the slope is constant
  • Point-Slope Derivation: Using the definition of slope with a known point
  • Geometric Interpretation: Slope represents the tangent of the angle the line makes with the x-axis
  • Special Cases: Horizontal lines (m = 0), vertical lines (m = undefined)
  • Applications: Used in physics for velocity, economics for marginal rates, engineering for gradients
\begin{aligned} \text{Derivation:} \\ \text{If slope m = } \frac{y - y_1}{x - x_1} \text{ for any point (x,y) on the line,} \\ \text{then: } y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \\ \text{This is the point-slope form.} \end{aligned}

Practice Problems

Problem 1

Find the equation of the line passing through the point (3, 4) with slope 2 in point-slope form.

Solution:

Using point-slope form with point (3,4) and slope m=2:

y - 4 = 2(x - 3)

Result: y - 4 = 2(x - 3)

Problem 2

Find the equation of the line passing through (-2, 5) with slope -1/2.

Solution:

Using point-slope form with point (-2,5) and slope m=-1/2:

y - 5 = (-1/2)(x - (-2))

y - 5 = (-1/2)(x + 2)

Result: y - 5 = (-1/2)(x + 2)

Problem 3

Convert the equation y + 3 = 2(x - 1) to slope-intercept form.

Solution:

Starting with: y + 3 = 2(x - 1)

y + 3 = 2x - 2

y = 2x - 2 - 3

y = 2x - 5

Result: y = 2x - 5

Key Takeaways
  • Point-slope form y - y₁ = m(x - x₁) uses a known point and slope to define a line
  • Slope m represents the rate of change (rise over run) of the line
  • Point-slope form can be converted to slope-intercept form y = mx + b
  • Slope can be positive (rising), negative (falling), zero (horizontal), or undefined (vertical)
  • Two points determine a unique line with slope m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁)
  • Point-slope form is particularly useful for tangent lines and rate problems
  • The equation represents all points (x,y) that satisfy the linear relationship
  • Slope is constant for any straight line (linear relationship)
