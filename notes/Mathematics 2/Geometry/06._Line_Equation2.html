Equation of a Line (Two-Point Form)
The two-point form finds the equation of a line passing through two given points in the coordinate plane.
General Case: $$y - y_1 = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}(x - x_1)$$
$$\text{When } x_1 \neq x_2$$
Vertical Line: $$x = x_1$$
$$\text{When } x_1 = x_2$$
Slope Formula: $$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$
$$\text{Change in y over change in x}$$
The two-point form is particularly useful when you know two points on the line. For non-vertical lines, first calculate the slope using the slope formula, then use point-slope form. Vertical lines have undefined slope and are represented by x = constant.
Different Cases of Two-Point Form

General Case (Non-Vertical)

Line through two distinct points
$$y - y_1 = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}(x - x_1)$$
Conditions: x₁ ≠ x₂
Slope: Defined and finite
Equation: Linear with non-zero slope
Examples: Rising, falling, or horizontal lines

Vertical Line Case

Vertical line through points
$$x = x_1$$
Conditions: x₁ = x₂
Slope: Undefined (∞)
Equation: x = constant
Examples: Parallel to y-axis

Horizontal Line Case

Horizontal line through points
$$y = y_1$$
Conditions: y₁ = y₂
Slope: Zero (0)
Equation: y = constant
Examples: Parallel to x-axis

Mathematical Foundation

The two-point form is derived from the fundamental properties of linear equations:

  • Line Uniqueness: Two distinct points determine exactly one straight line
  • Slope Calculation: The slope between two points is constant and represents the rate of change
  • Point-Slope Form: Once slope is known, use point-slope form with either point
  • Vertical Lines: When x-coordinates are equal, slope is undefined and equation is x = constant
  • Horizontal Lines: When y-coordinates are equal, slope is zero and equation is y = constant
  • Coordinate Geometry: Foundation for analytic geometry and linear algebra
\begin{aligned} \text{Derivation:} \\ \text{Given points A(x₁,y₁) and B(x₂,y₂):} \\ \text{Slope m = } \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \\ \text{Using point-slope form with point A:} \\ y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \\ \text{Substitute m:} \\ y - y_1 = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}(x - x_1) \end{aligned}

Practice Problems

Problem 1

Find the equation of the line passing through the points (3, 4) and (7, 10).

Solution:

Calculate slope: m = (10-4)/(7-3) = 6/4 = 3/2

Using point (3,4): y - 4 = (3/2)(x - 3)

Result: y - 4 = (3/2)(x - 3)

Problem 2

Find the equation of the line passing through (5, 2) and (5, 9).

Solution:

Same x-coordinate (5), so vertical line

Result: x = 5

Problem 3

Find the equation of the line passing through (-2, 6) and (4, 6).

Solution:

Same y-coordinate (6), so horizontal line

Result: y = 6

Key Takeaways
  • Two-point form finds the equation of a line passing through two given points
  • For non-vertical lines: calculate slope m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁), then use point-slope form
  • Vertical lines occur when x₁ = x₂, equation is x = x₁ (undefined slope)
  • Horizontal lines occur when y₁ = y₂, equation is y = y₁ (zero slope)
  • Two distinct points always determine exactly one straight line
  • The formula works for any two points in the coordinate plane
  • Essential for coordinate geometry and linear equation problems
  • Can be converted to slope-intercept or standard form as needed
