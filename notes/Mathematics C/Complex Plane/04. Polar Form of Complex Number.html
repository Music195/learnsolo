Polar Form of a Complex Number

Core Concept: Polar Representation

The polar form represents complex numbers using their distance from the origin (modulus) and angle from the positive real axis (argument). This form is particularly powerful for multiplication, division, and finding powers and roots.

A complex number \( z = a + bi \) in polar form is:

z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)

where

r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}, \quad \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)
Key Components:
  • Modulus r: Distance from origin to the point
  • Argument θ: Angle from positive real axis (counterclockwise)
  • Principal argument: Usually in range (-π, π]
  • Euler's formula: z = re^(iθ) (advanced form)

Conversion Formulas

Rectangular to Polar
r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}
\theta = \begin{cases} \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{b}{a}\right) & \text{if } a > 0 \\ \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{b}{a}\right) + \pi & \text{if } a < 0, b \geq 0 \\ \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{b}{a}\right) - \pi & \text{if } a < 0, b < 0 \\ \frac{\pi}{2} & \text{if } a = 0, b > 0 \\ -\frac{\pi}{2} & \text{if } a = 0, b < 0 \\ \text{undefined} & \text{if } a = 0, b = 0 \end{cases}
Polar to Rectangular
a = r\cos\theta
b = r\sin\theta

Geometric Interpretation

Polar Coordinates: The polar form directly corresponds to polar coordinates in the plane. The modulus r is the radial distance, and the argument θ is the angular displacement from the positive x-axis.
Visual Representation: Every complex number can be represented as a point (r, θ) in polar coordinates, or equivalently as a vector of length r at angle θ from the positive real axis.

Worked Examples

1

Convert z = 3 + 4i to polar form.

z = 3 + 4i, so a = 3, b = 4
r = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5
θ = tan⁻¹(b/a) = tan⁻¹(4/3) ≈ 0.927 radians (53.13°)
Polar form: z = 5(cos 0.927 + i sin 0.927)
Or approximately: z = 5(cos 53.13° + i sin 53.13°)
2

Convert z = 5(cos π/3 + i sin π/3) to rectangular form.

r = 5, θ = π/3 ≈ 1.047 radians (60°)
a = r cos θ = 5 × cos(π/3) = 5 × 0.5 = 2.5
b = r sin θ = 5 × sin(π/3) = 5 × (√3/2) ≈ 5 × 0.866 = 4.33
Rectangular form: z = 2.5 + 4.33i
Exact form: z = 2.5 + (5√3/2)i = 5/2 + (5√3/2)i
3

Find the polar form of z = -2 - 2i.

z = -2 - 2i, so a = -2, b = -2
r = √((-2)² + (-2)²) = √(4 + 4) = √8 = 2√2 ≈ 2.828
θ = tan⁻¹(b/a) = tan⁻¹((-2)/(-2)) = tan⁻¹(1) = π/4
Since a < 0 and b < 0, θ = π/4 - π = -3π/4
Polar form: z = 2√2(cos(-3π/4) + i sin(-3π/4))
This is in the third quadrant, 135° from positive x-axis

Applications and Advantages

Why Polar Form is Powerful
  • Multiplication: (r₁∠θ₁)(r₂∠θ₂) = r₁r₂∠(θ₁+θ₂)
  • Division: (r₁∠θ₁)/(r₂∠θ₂) = (r₁/r₂)∠(θ₁-θ₂)
  • Powers: De Moivre's theorem for complex powers
  • Roots: Finding nth roots of complex numbers
  • Rotation: Geometric interpretation of multiplication

Key Takeaways

  • Polar form: z = r(cos θ + i sin θ)
  • r = √(a² + b²), θ = tan⁻¹(b/a) with quadrant adjustments
  • Rectangular: a = r cos θ, b = r sin θ
  • Essential for complex multiplication and powers
  • Geometric interpretation using polar coordinates
  • Principal argument in range (-π, π]
