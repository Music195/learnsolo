Modulus and Argument of Product and Quotient

Core Concept: Properties of Modulus and Argument

The modulus and argument of products and quotients follow simple rules that make complex number calculations much easier. These properties are fundamental to understanding complex multiplication and division.

For any complex numbers z₁ and z₂:

1 \( |z₁z₂| = |z₁|\,|z₂| \quad \arg(z₁z₂) = \arg z₁ + \arg z₂ \)
2 \( \left|\dfrac{z₁}{z₂}\right| = \dfrac{|z₁|}{|z₂|} \quad \arg\left(\dfrac{z₁}{z₂}\right) = \arg z₁ - \arg z₂ \)
Key Properties:
  • Modulus of product: Product of individual moduli
  • Argument of product: Sum of individual arguments
  • Modulus of quotient: Division of individual moduli
  • Argument of quotient: Difference of individual arguments
  • Arguments are defined modulo 2π

Geometric Interpretation

Product Properties: When multiplying complex numbers, the resulting modulus is the product of individual moduli, and the argument is the sum of individual arguments. This corresponds to scaling by the product of magnitudes and rotating by the sum of angles.
Quotient Properties: When dividing complex numbers, the resulting modulus is the ratio of individual moduli, and the argument is the difference of individual arguments. This corresponds to scaling by the ratio of magnitudes and rotating by the difference of angles.
Why These Properties Work
  • Modulus: |z₁z₂| = √(a²+b²) where z₁z₂ = (a+bi), follows from polar multiplication
  • Argument: arg(z₁z₂) = arg(z₁) + arg(z₂) due to angle addition in rotation
  • Division: Similar logic applies with inverse operations
  • Periodicity: Arguments are defined modulo 2π (360°)

Worked Examples

1

Given |z₁| = 4, arg(z₁) = 60° and |z₂| = 2, arg(z₂) = 30°, find |z₁z₂| and arg(z₁z₂).

z₁ = 4∠60°, z₂ = 2∠30°
Modulus of product: |z₁z₂| = |z₁| × |z₂| = 4 × 2 = 8
Argument of product: arg(z₁z₂) = arg(z₁) + arg(z₂) = 60° + 30° = 90°
Therefore: z₁z₂ = 8∠90° = 8i
Verification: 4∠60° × 2∠30° = 8∠90° ✓
2

Given |z₁| = 6, arg(z₁) = 120° and |z₂| = 3, arg(z₂) = 45°, find |z₁/z₂| and arg(z₁/z₂).

z₁ = 6∠120°, z₂ = 3∠45°
Modulus of quotient: |z₁/z₂| = |z₁| / |z₂| = 6 / 3 = 2
Argument of quotient: arg(z₁/z₂) = arg(z₁) - arg(z₂) = 120° - 45° = 75°
Therefore: z₁/z₂ = 2∠75°
In rectangular form: 2(cos 75° + i sin 75°) ≈ 2(0.259 + i 0.965) ≈ 0.518 + 1.93i
3

If |z| = 1 and arg(z) = θ, find |z²| and arg(z²).

z = 1∠θ (unit circle)
z² = z × z = (1∠θ)(1∠θ) = 1∠(θ + θ) = 1∠(2θ)
Modulus of z²: |z²| = |z| × |z| = 1 × 1 = 1
Argument of z²: arg(z²) = arg(z) + arg(z) = θ + θ = 2θ
Therefore: z² = 1∠(2θ), which is also on the unit circle
This shows that squaring rotates the point by 2θ

Applications and Extensions

Important Applications
  • De Moivre's theorem: (r∠θ)ⁿ = rⁿ∠(nθ)
  • Complex roots: Finding nth roots of complex numbers
  • Trigonometric identities: Double and half angle formulas
  • Signal processing: Frequency domain analysis
  • Quantum mechanics: Phase factors and operators
  • Fractal geometry: Complex iteration and Julia sets

Key Takeaways

  • |z₁z₂| = |z₁| × |z₂| and arg(z₁z₂) = arg(z₁) + arg(z₂)
  • |z₁/z₂| = |z₁| / |z₂| and arg(z₁/z₂) = arg(z₁) - arg(z₂)
  • Arguments are defined modulo 2π (360°)
  • Geometric interpretation: scaling and rotation composition
  • Essential for De Moivre's theorem and complex roots
  • Fundamental to complex analysis and applications
