Vector Dot Product Properties

Core Properties of Dot Product

The dot product (scalar product) has several fundamental properties that make it a powerful tool in vector algebra and its applications.

Fundamental Properties
1. Commutative Property: \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}\)
2. Distributive Property: \(\vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) = \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} + \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c}\)
3. Scalar Multiplication: \((k\vec{a}) \cdot \vec{b} = k(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b})\)
4. Self Dot Product: \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{a} = |\vec{a}|^2\)
Key Insight: The dot product always produces a scalar (real number), not a vector. This distinguishes it from the cross product.

Mathematical Foundation

The properties of the dot product can be derived from its definition and the properties of real numbers.

Commutative Property Proof
\(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 = b_1a_1 + b_2a_2 = \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}\)
Order doesn't matter because multiplication is commutative in real numbers
Distributive Property Proof
\(\vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) = a_1(b_1 + c_1) + a_2(b_2 + c_2) = a_1b_1 + a_1c_1 + a_2b_2 + a_2c_2\)
\(= (a_1b_1 + a_2b_2) + (a_1c_1 + a_2c_2) = \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} + \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c}\)
Distributivity follows from the distributive property of real numbers
Scalar Multiplication Property
\((k\vec{a}) \cdot \vec{b} = (ka_1)b_1 + (ka_2)b_2 = k(a_1b_1 + a_2b_2) = k(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b})\)
Scalars can be factored out of dot products

Applications in Mathematics

Real-World Applications

Physics: Work done by a force, projection of vectors, energy calculations
Computer Graphics: Lighting calculations, normal vectors, collision detection
Linear Algebra: Orthogonal matrices, Gram-Schmidt process, vector projections
Statistics: Correlation coefficients, variance calculations, data projections

Practice Problems

Test your understanding with these additional practice problems.

1
Verify commutative property: \(\vec{u} = (5, -2)\), \(\vec{v} = (3, 7)\)
\(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 5×3 + (-2)×7 = 15 - 14 = 1\)
\(\vec{v} \cdot \vec{u} = 3×5 + 7×(-2) = 15 - 14 = 1\)
Property verified: both equal 1
2
Verify distributive property: \(\vec{p} = (1, 1)\), \(\vec{q} = (2, 3)\), \(\vec{r} = (4, 5)\)
Left: \((\vec{p} + \vec{q}) \cdot \vec{r} = (3, 4) \cdot (4, 5) = 3×4 + 4×5 = 12 + 20 = 32\)
Right: \(\vec{p} \cdot \vec{r} + \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r} = (1×4 + 1×5) + (2×4 + 3×5) = (4 + 5) + (8 + 15) = 9 + 23 = 32\)
Property verified: both equal 32
3
Verify scalar property: \(\vec{a} = (3, 4)\), \(\vec{b} = (1, 2)\), k = -2
Left: \((-2\vec{a}) \cdot \vec{b} = (-6, -8) \cdot (1, 2) = -6×1 + (-8)×2 = -6 - 16 = -22\)
Right: \(-2(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}) = -2(3×1 + 4×2) = -2(3 + 8) = -2×11 = -22\)
Property verified: both equal -22

Key Takeaways

  • The dot product is commutative: order doesn't matter
  • Dot product distributes over vector addition
  • Scalars can be factored out of dot products
  • Self dot product equals the square of the magnitude
  • Properties enable vector algebra manipulations
  • Essential for projections, angles, and orthogonality
