Conditions for Vector Perpendicularity

Mathematical Foundation

Two vectors are perpendicular (or orthogonal) if they form a right angle (90°) with each other. This is one of the most important concepts in vector algebra and has numerous applications in geometry, physics, and computer science.

Perpendicularity Conditions
For non-zero vectors \(\vec{a} = (a_1, a_2)\) and \(\vec{b} = (b_1, b_2)\):
\(\vec{a} \perp \vec{b} \iff \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0\)
\(\vec{a} \perp \vec{b} \iff a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 = 0\)
Key Properties
The zero vector is perpendicular to every vector
Perpendicularity is symmetric: if \(\vec{a} \perp \vec{b}\), then \(\vec{b} \perp \vec{a}\)
The angle between perpendicular vectors is exactly 90°

Geometric Interpretation

Perpendicular vectors form a right angle at their intersection point. In the coordinate plane, this means their direction vectors are at 90° to each other.

Right Angle Condition: θ = 90° ⇒ cosθ = 0 ⇒ \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0\)
  • Visual Test: Two vectors are perpendicular if their lines form a right angle
  • Dot Product Test: The dot product is zero for perpendicular vectors
  • Component Test: The sum of products of corresponding components is zero
  • Geometric Test: One vector is perpendicular to the normal of the other

    Component-wise Conditions

    The perpendicularity condition can be expressed in terms of vector components:

    2D Vectors
    \(\vec{a} = (a_1, a_2)\), \(\vec{b} = (b_1, b_2)\)
    Perpendicular if: \(a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 = 0\)
    3D Vectors
    \(\vec{a} = (a_1, a_2, a_3)\), \(\vec{b} = (b_1, b_2, b_3)\)
    Perpendicular if: \(a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3 = 0\)
    Standard Basis Vectors
    \(\vec{i} \perp \vec{j}\), \(\vec{i} \perp \vec{k}\), \(\vec{j} \perp \vec{k}\)
    Coordinate axes are mutually perpendicular

    Worked Examples

    1
    Determine if \(\vec{a} = (2, 3)\) and \(\vec{b} = (-3, 2)\) are perpendicular
    Dot product: \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 2 \times (-3) + 3 \times 2 = -6 + 6 = 0\)
    Since the dot product is 0, the vectors are perpendicular.
    2
    Find k such that \(\vec{u} = (k, 1)\) ⊥ \(\vec{v} = (2, 3)\)
    Perpendicularity condition: \(k \times 2 + 1 \times 3 = 0\)
    \(2k + 3 = 0\)
    \(2k = -3\), \(k = -\frac{3}{2}\)
    The value is \(k = -1.5\)
    3
    Show that vectors \(\vec{p} = (1, 1, 1)\) and \(\vec{q} = (1, -1, 0)\) are not perpendicular
    Dot product: \(\vec{p} \cdot \vec{q} = 1 \times 1 + 1 \times (-1) + 1 \times 0 = 1 - 1 + 0 = 0\)
    Wait, the dot product is 0, so they are actually perpendicular!
    Note: The problem statement was incorrect - these vectors are perpendicular.

    Applications

    Perpendicularity is fundamental in many mathematical and practical applications:

  • Coordinate Geometry: Determining right angles in shapes and figures
  • Physics: Work done by perpendicular forces, equilibrium conditions
  • Computer Graphics: Surface normals, lighting calculations
  • Linear Algebra: Orthogonal vectors, basis vectors
  • Engineering: Structural analysis, force decomposition

    • Practice Problems

    1
    Are \(\vec{a} = (4, 2)\) and \(\vec{b} = (-1, 2)\) perpendicular?
    \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 4 \times (-1) + 2 \times 2 = -4 + 4 = 0\)
    Yes, the vectors are perpendicular.
    2
    Find m so that \(\vec{u} = (m, 3)\) ⊥ \(\vec{v} = (2, 4)\)
    \(m \times 2 + 3 \times 4 = 0\)
    \(2m + 12 = 0\), \(2m = -12\), \(m = -6\)
    3
    Determine if \(\vec{p} = (1, 0, 1)\) and \(\vec{q} = (0, 1, -1)\) are perpendicular
    \(\vec{p} \cdot \vec{q} = 1 \times 0 + 0 \times 1 + 1 \times (-1) = 0 + 0 - 1 = -1 ≠ 0\)
    No, the vectors are not perpendicular.

    Key Takeaways

    • Two vectors are perpendicular if their dot product equals zero
    • In component form: \(a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 = 0\) for 2D vectors
    • The angle between perpendicular vectors is exactly 90°
    • The zero vector is perpendicular to all vectors
    • Perpendicularity is symmetric and reflexive
    • This concept is essential for coordinate geometry and physics
