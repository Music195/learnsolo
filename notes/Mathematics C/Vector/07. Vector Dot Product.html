Vector Dot Product

Mathematical Foundation

The dot product (also called scalar product) is an operation that takes two vectors and returns a scalar. It measures how much one vector "goes in the direction" of another vector.

Dot Product Definition
For vectors \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) with angle \(\theta\) between them:
\(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = |\vec{a}| |\vec{b}| \cos\theta\)
In component form: \(\vec{a} = (a_1, a_2)\), \(\vec{b} = (b_1, b_2)\)
\(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2\)
Special Cases
If \(\vec{a} = \vec{0}\) or \(\vec{b} = \vec{0}\), then \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0\)
If \(\theta = 90^\circ\), then \(\cos\theta = 0\) and \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0\) (perpendicular vectors)
If \(\theta = 0^\circ\), then \(\cos\theta = 1\) and \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = |\vec{a}| |\vec{b}|\) (parallel vectors)

Properties of Dot Product

Commutative Property
\(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}\)
Order doesn't matter in dot product
Distributive Property
\(\vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) = \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} + \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c}\)
Dot product distributes over vector addition
Scalar Multiplication
\((k\vec{a}) \cdot \vec{b} = k(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b})\)
Scalars can be factored out
Self Dot Product
\(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{a} = |\vec{a}|^2\)
Square of the magnitude

Geometric Interpretation

The dot product has a clear geometric meaning: it represents the projection of one vector onto another, multiplied by the magnitude of the second vector.

Projection: \(\text{proj}_{\vec{b}}\vec{a} = \frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{|\vec{b}|^2} \vec{b}\)
  • Acute Angle (0° < θ < 90°): Dot product is positive
  • Right Angle (θ = 90°): Dot product is zero
  • Obtuse Angle (90° < θ < 180°): Dot product is negative
  • Parallel Vectors: |\(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}\)| = |\(\vec{a}\)| |\(\vec{b}\)|
  • Perpendicular Vectors: \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0\)

    • Worked Examples

    1
    Calculate \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}\) where \(\vec{a} = (2, 3)\) and \(\vec{b} = (4, 1)\)
    Component form: \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 2 \times 4 + 3 \times 1\)
    Calculation: \(8 + 3 = 11\)
    The dot product is 11.
    2
    Find the angle between \(\vec{u} = (1, 2)\) and \(\vec{v} = (3, 4)\)
    First calculate dot product: \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 1 \times 3 + 2 \times 4 = 3 + 8 = 11\)
    Magnitudes: \(|\vec{u}| = \sqrt{1^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{5}\), \(|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{25} = 5\)
    Angle: \(\cos\theta = \frac{11}{\sqrt{5} \times 5} = \frac{11}{5\sqrt{5}}\)
    \(\theta = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{11}{5\sqrt{5}}\right) \approx 12.09^\circ\)
    3
    Determine if vectors \(\vec{p} = (1, 1)\) and \(\vec{q} = (-1, 1)\) are perpendicular
    Dot product: \(\vec{p} \cdot \vec{q} = 1 \times (-1) + 1 \times 1 = -1 + 1 = 0\)
    Since the dot product is 0, the vectors are perpendicular.

    Applications

    Dot products are fundamental in many areas of mathematics, physics, and computer science:

  • Physics: Work done by a force, power calculations
  • Computer Graphics: Lighting calculations, determining surface normals
  • Machine Learning: Cosine similarity, feature comparison
  • Engineering: Stress analysis, structural calculations
  • Navigation: Determining direction and orientation

    • Practice Problems

    1
    Compute \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}\) for \(\vec{a} = (5, -2)\) and \(\vec{b} = (3, 7)\)
    \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 5 \times 3 + (-2) \times 7 = 15 - 14 = 1\)
    2
    Find \(2\vec{u} \cdot 3\vec{v}\) where \(\vec{u} = (1, 4)\) and \(\vec{v} = (2, -1)\)
    First: \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 1 \times 2 + 4 \times (-1) = 2 - 4 = -2\)
    Then: \(2\vec{u} \cdot 3\vec{v} = 2 \times 3 \times (\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v}) = 6 \times (-2) = -12\)
    3
    Show that vectors \(\vec{m} = (3, 1)\) and \(\vec{n} = (1, -3)\) are perpendicular
    \(\vec{m} \cdot \vec{n} = 3 \times 1 + 1 \times (-3) = 3 - 3 = 0\)
    Dot product is zero, so vectors are perpendicular.

    Key Takeaways

    • The dot product returns a scalar, not a vector
    • Component form: \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2\)
    • Geometric form: \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = |\vec{a}| |\vec{b}| \cos\theta\)
    • Perpendicular vectors have zero dot product
    • Dot product is commutative and distributive
    • Self dot product gives the square of the magnitude
