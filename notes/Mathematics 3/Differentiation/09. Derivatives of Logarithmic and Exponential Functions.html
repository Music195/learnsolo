Derivatives of Logarithmic and Exponential Functions

Basic Derivatives

Exponential Functions

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[a^x] = a^x \ln a \quad (a > 0, a \neq 1)$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{f(x)}] = e^{f(x)} \cdot f'(x)$

Logarithmic Functions

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \dfrac{1}{x} \quad (x > 0)$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\log_a x] = \dfrac{1}{x \ln a} \quad (x > 0, a > 0, a \neq 1)$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln f(x)] = \dfrac{f'(x)}{f(x)} \quad (f(x) > 0)$

Understanding Logarithmic and Exponential Derivatives

Exponential and logarithmic functions are inverses of each other, and their derivatives reflect this fundamental relationship. The exponential function grows at a rate proportional to its current value, while the logarithmic function's rate of change is inversely proportional to its argument.

Key Relationships:

  • Exponential Growth: $e^x$ has the remarkable property that its derivative equals itself
  • Logarithmic Decay: The derivative of $\ln x$ is $1/x$, showing that logarithmic functions change more slowly as x increases
  • Chain Rule Connection: For composite functions, both exponential and logarithmic derivatives multiply by the inner function's derivative
  • Base Conversion: Changing bases in logarithms affects the derivative by a constant factor

Examples

Example 1: Derivative of e^x

Find $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x]$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x$
The exponential function has the unique property that its derivative equals itself. This is why e is called the natural base.

Example 2: Derivative of ln(x)

Find $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln x]$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \dfrac{1}{x}$
The natural logarithm's derivative is 1/x. At x = 1: ln(1) = 0, and the slope is 1. As x increases, the slope decreases.

Example 3: Derivative of e^(2x)

Find $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{2x}]$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{2x}] = e^{2x} \cdot 2 = 2e^{2x}$
Using chain rule: derivative of e^u where u = 2x is e^u · du/dx = e^(2x) · 2

Example 4: Derivative of ln(x² + 1)

Find $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln(x^2 + 1)]$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln(x^2 + 1)] = \dfrac{1}{x^2 + 1} \cdot \dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2 + 1] = \dfrac{2x}{x^2 + 1}$
Using chain rule: derivative of ln(u) where u = x² + 1 is (1/u) · du/dx = 1/(x² + 1) · 2x

Example 5: Derivative of x^x

Find $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^x]$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^x] = x^x (\ln x + 1)$
First write as exponential: x^x = e^(x ln x), then derivative is e^(x ln x) · d/dx(x ln x) = x^x · (ln x + x · 1/x) = x^x (ln x + 1)

Example 6: Derivative of 2^(x²)

Find $\dfrac{d}{dx}[2^{x^2}]$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[2^{x^2}] = 2^{x^2} \ln 2 \cdot 2x = 2x \cdot 2^{x^2} \ln 2$
Using chain rule: derivative of a^u where a = 2, u = x² is a^u · ln a · du/dx = 2^(x²) · ln 2 · 2x

Derivation of Key Formulas

Derivative of e^x

Using the limit definition and the fact that e is defined as the number where this limit equals 1:

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x] = \lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{e^{x+h} - e^x}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{e^x (e^h - 1)}{h} = e^x \lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{e^h - 1}{h} = e^x \cdot 1 = e^x$

Derivative of ln(x)

Using implicit differentiation. Let y = ln x, so e^y = x. Differentiate both sides:

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^y] = \dfrac{d}{dx}[x]$

$e^y \cdot \dfrac{dy}{dx} = 1$

$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{1}{e^y} = \dfrac{1}{x}$

Derivative of a^x

Write a^x = e^(x ln a), then differentiate:

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[a^x] = \dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{x \ln a}] = e^{x \ln a} \cdot \ln a = a^x \ln a$

Applications

Population Growth

Bacterial growth often follows P(t) = P₀e^(kt). The growth rate dP/dt = kP shows exponential growth proportional to current population.

Radioactive Decay

Radioactive substances decay as N(t) = N₀e^(-λt). The decay rate dN/dt = -λN is proportional to the remaining amount.

pH and Chemistry

pH = -log₁₀[H⁺]. The derivative dpH/d[H⁺] = -1/(ln(10)[H⁺]) shows how pH changes with concentration.

Finance: Compound Interest

Compound interest A = Pe^(rt). The instantaneous growth rate dA/dt = rA shows exponential wealth growth.

Physics: Temperature and Heat

Newton's law of cooling: dT/dt = -k(T - T₀). Logarithmic relationships appear in heat transfer problems.

Information Theory

Entropy and information content often involve logarithmic derivatives in signal processing and data compression.

Practice Problems

Problem 1

Find the derivative: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{3x} + 2^x]$

Show Solution

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{3x} + 2^x] = 3e^{3x} + 2^x \ln 2$

Problem 2

Find the derivative: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln(2x + 1)]$

Show Solution

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln(2x + 1)] = \dfrac{1}{2x + 1} \cdot 2 = \dfrac{2}{2x + 1}$

Problem 3

Find the derivative: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2 e^x]$

Show Solution

Using product rule: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2 e^x] = x^2 e^x + e^x \cdot 2x = e^x (x^2 + 2x)$

Problem 4

Find the derivative: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\dfrac{\ln x}{x^2}]$

Show Solution

Using quotient rule: $\dfrac{\dfrac{1}{x} \cdot x^2 - \ln x \cdot 2x}{x^4} = \dfrac{x - 2x \ln x}{x^4} = \dfrac{1 - 2\ln x}{x^3}$

Problem 5

Find the derivative: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{\sin x}]$

Show Solution

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{\sin x}] = e^{\sin x} \cdot \cos x$

Problem 6

Find the derivative: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln(\cos x)]$

Show Solution

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln(\cos x)] = \dfrac{1}{\cos x} \cdot (-\sin x) = -\tan x$

Key Takeaways

Exponential Magic

$e^x$ is the only function whose derivative equals itself. This makes it fundamental in calculus and science.

Logarithmic Rate

The derivative of $\ln x$ is $1/x$, showing that logarithmic functions change more slowly as x increases.

Chain Rule Essential

For composite functions like $e^{f(x)}$ or $\ln f(x)$, always multiply by f'(x) - the chain rule is crucial here.

Base Matters

When differentiating $a^x$, you get an extra factor of $\ln a$. For base e, this factor is 1, which is why e is "natural."

Real-World Importance

These derivatives appear everywhere: population growth, radioactive decay, compound interest, pH calculations, and many physical laws.
