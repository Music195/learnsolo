Derivative Notation
Derivative Notation
The Fundamental Definition:
$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$
1. Leibniz Notation (Most Common)
$\dfrac{dy}{dx}$: "The derivative of y with respect to x"
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[f(x)]$: "The derivative of f(x) with respect to x"
$\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^2) = 2x$
2. Lagrange Notation (Prime Notation)
$f'(x)$: "f prime of x"
$y'$: "y prime"
$(x^2)' = 2x$
3. Higher-Order Derivatives
Second Derivative:
$\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$, $f''(x)$, $y''$

Third Derivative:
$\dfrac{d^3y}{dx^3}$, $f'''(x)$, $y'''$

$n$th Derivative:
$\dfrac{d^ny}{dx^n}$, $f^{(n)}(x)$
4. Partial Derivatives
$\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}$: Partial derivative of f with respect to x
$\dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}$: Second partial derivative
$\dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y}$: Mixed partial derivative

6. When to Use Each Notation
  • Leibniz ($\dfrac{dy}{dx}$): When emphasizing the relationship between variables
  • Lagrange ($f'(x)$): When working with specific functions
  • Newton ($\dot{y}$): In physics for time derivatives
  • Partial ($\dfrac{\partial}{\partial x}$): In multivariable calculus
7. Examples with Different Notations
Power Rule:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1}$
$(x^n)' = nx^{n-1}$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}x^3 = 3x^2$
Chain Rule:
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy}{du} \cdot \dfrac{du}{dx}$
$f'(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$
Product Rule:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[uv] = u'v + uv'$
$(uv)' = u'v + uv'$
8. Applications
Physics: Velocity ($\dfrac{dx}{dt}$), acceleration ($\dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}$)
Economics: Marginal cost ($\dfrac{dC}{dQ}$), marginal revenue ($\dfrac{dR}{dQ}$)
Engineering: Rate of change in systems analysis

Problem 1: Write $\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^4)$ in Lagrange notation.
Problem 2: Express $f''(x)$ using Leibniz notation.
Problem 3: What does $\dfrac{\partial^2 z}{\partial x \partial y}$ represent?
10. Key Takeaways
  • Multiple notations exist for the same mathematical concept
  • Choose notation based on context and clarity
  • Leibniz notation shows variable relationships
  • Lagrange notation is function-specific
  • Higher derivatives use superscripts or multiple d's
  • Partial derivatives use ∂ (curly d) symbol
