Properties of Infinite Series

üìö Core Concepts

Infinite series are sums of infinitely many terms. When we work with convergent series, we can manipulate them algebraically much like finite sums, but with important caveats about convergence.

For infinite series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n$ and $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n$ that both converge to sums S and T respectively, the following properties hold:
Important: These properties only apply when both series converge. If either series diverges, the properties generally do not hold.

üìä Fundamental Properties

1. Scalar Multiplication

$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} k a_n = k \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n = kS$

Multiplying each term by a constant k multiplies the sum by k.

Requires: $\sum a_n$ converges

2. Addition & Subtraction

$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (a_n + b_n) = S + T$
$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (a_n - b_n) = S - T$

Can add or subtract corresponding terms of convergent series.

Requires: Both series converge

3. Term-by-Term Operations

$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} c a_n = cS$ (c constant)
$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (c a_n + d b_n) = cS + dT$

Linear combinations of convergent series converge to the same linear combination of their sums.

Requires: All series converge

4. Partial Sums

If $\sum a_n = S$, then $S_n ‚Üí S$
where $S_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k$

The sequence of partial sums converges to the series sum.

This is the definition of convergence

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Caveats

  • Convergence Required: These properties only work for convergent series. Divergent series don't behave predictably under algebraic operations.
  • Term-by-Term Limits: You cannot always take limits term by term: $\lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k \neq \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \lim_{n\to\infty} a_k$ in general.
  • Absolute Convergence: Absolutely convergent series can be rearranged arbitrarily, but conditionally convergent series cannot.
  • Multiplication: The Cauchy product of two series may converge even if the original series diverge.

üí° Examples

Example 1: Given $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{2^n} = 1$ and $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{4^n} = \dfrac{1}{3}$, find $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(2 \cdot \dfrac{1}{2^n} + 3 \cdot \dfrac{1}{4^n}\right)$.

Using properties: $2 \cdot 1 + 3 \cdot \dfrac{1}{3} = 2 + 1 = 3$.
Example 2: If $\sum a_n = 2$ and $\sum b_n = 3$, what is $\sum (a_n - b_n)$?

Using subtraction property: $2 - 3 = -1$.

üî¨ Applications

  • Power Series: Manipulating Taylor series expansions
  • Fourier Series: Combining trigonometric series
  • Generating Functions: Solving recurrence relations
  • Probability: Working with infinite probability distributions
  • Physics: Series solutions to differential equations

üìù Practice Problems

Problem 1: Given $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{3^n} = \dfrac{1}{2}$ and $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{5^n} = \dfrac{1}{4}$, find $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(\dfrac{1}{3^n} + \dfrac{1}{5^n}\right)$.

Using addition property: $\dfrac{1}{2} + \dfrac{1}{4} = \dfrac{3}{4}$.
Problem 2: If $\sum a_n = 5$, find $\sum 3a_n$.

Using scalar multiplication: $3 \times 5 = 15$.
Problem 3: Given $\sum p_n = 7$ and $\sum q_n = 4$, find $\sum (2p_n - q_n)$.

Using properties: $2 \times 7 - 4 = 14 - 4 = 10$.
Problem 4: Explain why $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n$ diverges, so we cannot apply these properties to it.

The harmonic series $\sum \dfrac{1}{n}$ diverges, and $\sum n$ grows even faster, so it definitely diverges. These properties only apply to convergent series.

üéØ Key Takeaways

  • Series properties only apply to convergent series
  • Scalar multiplication: multiply sum by constant
  • Addition/subtraction: combine sums of convergent series
  • Linear combinations preserve convergence
  • Divergent series don't follow these rules
  • Absolute convergence allows rearrangement
  • Properties are essential for series manipulation
