1. Convergence and Divergence of Sequences

üìê Concepts

A sequence \( \{a_n\} \) is a function whose domain is the set of positive integers. The limit of a sequence describes the behavior of the sequence as n approaches infinity.

‚úÖ Convergence

A sequence converges to a limit L if for every Œµ > 0, there exists N such that for all n > N, |a_n - L| < Œµ.

\( \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L \)

‚ùå Divergence

A sequence diverges if it does not converge to any finite limit. Divergence can occur in three ways:

  • To +‚àû: \( \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = +\infty \)
  • To -‚àû: \( \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = -\infty \)
  • Oscillation: No limit exists (sequence oscillates)
Key Distinction: Convergence means approaching a specific finite value, while divergence means not approaching any finite value (could go to infinity or oscillate).

üîç Examples

Example 1: Convergent Sequence

Consider the sequence \( a_n = \frac{1}{n} \)

As n increases: 1, 0.5, 0.333..., 0.25, 0.2, 0.166...

This sequence approaches 0: \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} = 0 \)

Example 2: Divergent to +‚àû

Consider the sequence \( a_n = n \)

As n increases: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7...

This sequence grows without bound: \( \lim_{n \to \infty} n = +\infty \)

Example 3: Divergent to -‚àû

Consider the sequence \( a_n = -n \)

As n increases: -1, -2, -3, -4, -5, -6...

This sequence decreases without bound: \( \lim_{n \to \infty} -n = -\infty \)

Example 4: Oscillating Sequence

Consider the sequence \( a_n = (-1)^n \)

As n increases: 1, -1, 1, -1, 1, -1...

This sequence oscillates between 1 and -1 with no limit.

‚úèÔ∏è Practice Problems

Problem 1: Determine whether the sequence \( a_n = \frac{3n + 1}{2n - 1} \) converges or diverges. If it converges, find the limit.

Divide numerator and denominator by n:

\( a_n = \frac{3n + 1}{2n - 1} = \frac{3 + \frac{1}{n}}{2 - \frac{1}{n}} \)

As \( n \to \infty \): \( \frac{3 + 0}{2 - 0} = \frac{3}{2} \)

Therefore, \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{3n + 1}{2n - 1} = \frac{3}{2} \)
Problem 2: Determine whether the sequence \( a_n = (-1)^n \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \) converges or diverges.

The sequence takes values: \( \frac{1}{\sqrt{1}}, -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}, -\frac{1}{\sqrt{4}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}, \dots \)

The absolute values approach 0, but the signs alternate.

Consider subsequences:

  • Even n: \( a_{2k} = (-1)^{2k} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2k}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2k}} \to 0^+ \)
  • Odd n: \( a_{2k+1} = (-1)^{2k+1} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2k+1}} = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2k+1}} \to 0^- \)

The subsequences converge to different limits (0 and 0), but the sequence itself oscillates and does not converge to a single limit.

Therefore, the sequence diverges (oscillates).

Problem 3: Determine whether the sequence \( a_n = \frac{n!}{n^n} \) converges or diverges.

Take the natural logarithm: \( \ln a_n = \ln(n!) - n \ln n = \sum_{k=1}^n \ln k - n \ln n \)

Using Stirling's approximation: \( n! \approx \sqrt{2\pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n \)

\( \ln a_n \approx \ln(\sqrt{2\pi n}) + n \ln n - n \ln e - n \ln n = \ln(\sqrt{2\pi n}) - n \)

\( = \frac{1}{2} \ln(2\pi n) - n \to -\infty \) as \( n \to \infty \)

Since \( \ln a_n \to -\infty \), then \( a_n \to 0^+ \).

Therefore, the sequence converges to 0.

üéØ Key Takeaways

  • Convergence: Sequence approaches a specific finite value L
  • Divergence to +‚àû: Sequence grows without bound
  • Divergence to -‚àû: Sequence decreases without bound
  • Oscillation: Sequence alternates without approaching any value
  • Convergent sequences are bounded, but bounded sequences may not converge
  • The limit of a convergent sequence is unique
  • Sequences can converge to 0 even if they oscillate (like (-1)^n/n)
