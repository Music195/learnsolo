Limit of sin(x)/x
$\lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{\sin x}{x} = 1$

The limit of sin(x)/x as x approaches 0 is one of the most important limits in calculus. This result is fundamental to the derivative of the sine function and appears in many trigonometric limits and integrals.

This limit is crucial because:

üìê Geometric Proof (Squeeze Theorem)

Unit Circle Geometry

The most elegant proof uses the unit circle and the squeeze theorem. Consider an angle Œ∏ in the unit circle:

Step 1: In the unit circle, we have a sector of angle Œ∏ and an isosceles triangle with two sides of length 1.

Area of sector: $\dfrac{1}{2} \theta$ (for small Œ∏)
Area of triangle: $\dfrac{1}{2} \sin \theta \cdot 1 = \dfrac{1}{2} \sin \theta$
Area of segment: $\dfrac{1}{2} \tan \theta$ (approximately, for small Œ∏)
Step 2: For Œ∏ between 0 and œÄ/2, we have the inequality:

Area of triangle ‚â§ Area of sector ‚â§ Area of segment

$\dfrac{1}{2} \sin \theta \leq \dfrac{1}{2} \theta \leq \dfrac{1}{2} \tan \theta$
Step 3: Dividing by 1/2 and sin Œ∏ (which is positive):

$1 \leq \dfrac{\theta}{\sin \theta} \leq \dfrac{1}{\cos \theta}$

Taking reciprocals (reversing inequalities): $\cos \theta \leq \dfrac{\sin \theta}{\theta} \leq 1$
Step 4: As Œ∏ ‚Üí 0, cos Œ∏ ‚Üí 1, so by the squeeze theorem:

$\lim_{\theta \to 0} \dfrac{\sin \theta}{\theta} = 1$

üîç Squeeze Theorem Proof

Direct Squeeze Theorem Application

Using the inequalities derived from geometry:

For 0 < Œ∏ < œÄ/2:    cos Œ∏ ‚â§ sin Œ∏ / Œ∏ ‚â§ 1

As Œ∏ approaches 0:

  • The left side approaches 1
  • The right side approaches 1
  • Therefore, the middle expression is "squeezed" to approach 1

This works for Œ∏ approaching 0 from either side due to the even function property of sin(x)/x.

üìù Practice Problems

Problem 1: Evaluate $\lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{\sin 3x}{x}$.

Let u = 3x, so as x‚Üí0, u‚Üí0. Then: $\lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{\sin 3x}{x} = \lim_{u \to 0} \dfrac{\sin u}{u/3} = 3 \lim_{u \to 0} \dfrac{\sin u}{u} = 3 \cdot 1 = 3$.
Problem 2: Find $\lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{1 - \cos x}{x^2}$.

Multiply numerator and denominator by (1 + cos x): $\lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{1 - \cos^2 x}{x^2(1 + \cos x)} = \lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{\sin^2 x}{x^2(1 + \cos x)} = \lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{\sin x}{x} \cdot \dfrac{\sin x}{x} \cdot \dfrac{1}{1 + \cos x} = 1 \cdot 1 \cdot \dfrac{1}{2} = \dfrac{1}{2}$.
Problem 3: Evaluate $\lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{\tan x}{x}$.

$\dfrac{\tan x}{x} = \dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x \cdot x} = \dfrac{\sin x}{x} \cdot \dfrac{1}{\cos x}$. As x‚Üí0, this becomes 1 √ó 1/1 = 1.
Problem 4: Find $\lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{\sin 5x}{\sin 2x}$.

$\dfrac{\sin 5x}{\sin 2x} = \dfrac{\sin 5x}{5x} \cdot \dfrac{2x}{\sin 2x} \cdot \dfrac{5x}{2x} = \dfrac{5}{2} \cdot \dfrac{\sin 5x}{5x} \cdot \dfrac{2x}{\sin 2x}$. As x‚Üí0, this becomes (5/2) √ó 1 √ó 1 = 5/2.
