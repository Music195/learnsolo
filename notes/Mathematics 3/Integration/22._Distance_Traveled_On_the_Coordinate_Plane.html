Distance Traveled on the Coordinate Plane

üìê Concepts

When a point moves along a curve in the coordinate plane with parametric equations \( x = x(t) \), \( y = y(t) \), the distance traveled (arc length) between times \( t_1 \) and \( t_2 \) is given by:

\( s = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2} \, dt \)

This formula represents the speed of the particle at each instant, which is the magnitude of the velocity vector \( \vec{v} = \langle \frac{dx}{dt}, \frac{dy}{dt} \rangle \).

Geometric Interpretation: The arc length integral sums up infinitesimal distances \( ds = \sqrt{(dx)^2 + (dy)^2} \) along the curve, similar to the Pythagorean theorem for each tiny segment.

üîç Examples

Example 1: Straight Line

A particle moves along the line \( y = x \) from (0,0) to (1,1). If \( x = t \), \( y = t \), find the distance traveled from t=0 to t=1.

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = 1, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = 1 \)

\( s = \int_{0}^{1} \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} \, dt = \int_{0}^{1} \sqrt{2} \, dt = \sqrt{2} \)

Example 2: Circle

A point moves around a circle of radius r: \( x = r\cos t \), \( y = r\sin t \). Find distance for one full revolution (t from 0 to 2œÄ).

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = -r\sin t, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = r\cos t \)

\( s = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sqrt{(-r\sin t)^2 + (r\cos t)^2} \, dt = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sqrt{r^2(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t)} \, dt \)

\( = \int_{0}^{2\pi} r \, dt = r \cdot 2\pi = 2\pi r \) (circumference)

Example 3: Parabolic Path

A projectile follows \( x = t \), \( y = -\frac{1}{2}gt^2 + v_0 t \). Find distance from t=0 to t=T.

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = 1, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = -gt + v_0 \)

\( s = \int_{0}^{T} \sqrt{1^2 + (-gt + v_0)^2} \, dt \)

üöÄ Applications

  • Physics: Calculating distance traveled by objects moving in two dimensions (projectiles, planetary orbits)
  • Engineering: Determining cable lengths, path planning for robots, material usage calculations
  • Computer Graphics: Computing curve lengths for animation and rendering
  • Navigation: GPS path distance calculations, flight path optimization
  • Manufacturing: Calculating material requirements for curved components

Problem 1: Find the distance traveled by a particle with parametric equations \( x = t^2 \), \( y = 2t \) from t=0 to t=2.

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = 2t, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = 2 \)

\( s = \int_{0}^{2} \sqrt{(2t)^2 + 2^2} \, dt = \int_{0}^{2} \sqrt{4t^2 + 4} \, dt = \int_{0}^{2} \sqrt{4(t^2 + 1)} \, dt \)

\( = \int_{0}^{2} 2\sqrt{t^2 + 1} \, dt = 2\left[\frac{1}{2}t\sqrt{t^2 + 1} + \frac{1}{2}\ln|t + \sqrt{t^2 + 1}|\right]_{0}^{2} \)

\( = \left[t\sqrt{t^2 + 1} + \ln|t + \sqrt{t^2 + 1}|\right]_{0}^{2} = (2\sqrt{5} + \ln(2 + \sqrt{5})) - (0 + \ln 1) \)

\( = 2\sqrt{5} + \ln(2 + \sqrt{5}) \approx 4.47 + 1.44 = 5.91 \)
Problem 2: A cycloid is given by \( x = t - \sin t \), \( y = 1 - \cos t \). Find the arc length for one arch (t from 0 to 2œÄ).

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = 1 - \cos t, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = \sin t \)

\( s = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sqrt{(1 - \cos t)^2 + (\sin t)^2} \, dt = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sqrt{1 - 2\cos t + \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t} \, dt \)

\( = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sqrt{2 - 2\cos t} \, dt = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sqrt{4 \cdot \frac{1 - \cos t}{2}} \, dt = \int_{0}^{2\pi} 2\sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos t}{2}} \, dt \)

\( = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sqrt{2(1 - \cos t)} \, dt = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{1 - \cos t} \, dt \)

Using the identity \( 1 - \cos t = 2\sin^2(t/2) \):

\( s = \sqrt{2} \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sqrt{2\sin^2(t/2)} \, dt = \sqrt{2} \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sqrt{2} |\sin(t/2)| \, dt = 2 \int_{0}^{2\pi} |\sin(t/2)| \, dt \)

\( = 2 \int_{0}^{2\pi} \sin(t/2) \, dt \) (since sin(t/2) ‚â• 0 for t ‚àà [0, 2œÄ])

\( = 2 \left[-2\cos(t/2)\right]_{0}^{2\pi} = 2(-2)(\cos \pi - \cos 0) = -4(-1 - 1) = -4(-2) = 8 \)
Problem 3: Find the length of the curve \( x = \cos^3 t \), \( y = \sin^3 t \) (astroid) from t=0 to t=œÄ/2.

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = 3\cos^2 t (-\sin t) = -3\cos^2 t \sin t \)
\( \frac{dy}{dt} = 3\sin^2 t \cos t \)

\( s = \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sqrt{(-3\cos^2 t \sin t)^2 + (3\sin^2 t \cos t)^2} \, dt \)

\( = \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sqrt{9\cos^4 t \sin^2 t + 9\sin^4 t \cos^2 t} \, dt = \int_{0}^{\pi/2} 3|\cos t \sin t| \sqrt{\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t} \, dt \)

\( = \int_{0}^{\pi/2} 3|\cos t \sin t| \cdot 1 \, dt = 3 \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \cos t \sin t \, dt \) (since both ‚â• 0 in this interval)

\( = 3 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sin 2t \, dt = \frac{3}{2} \left[-\frac{1}{2}\cos 2t\right]_{0}^{\pi/2} = -\frac{3}{4} [\cos 2t]_{0}^{\pi/2} \)

\( = -\frac{3}{4} (\cos \pi - \cos 0) = -\frac{3}{4} (-1 - 1) = -\frac{3}{4} (-2) = \frac{6}{4} = \frac{3}{2} \)

üéØ Key Takeaways

  • Arc length formula: \( s = \int \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2} \, dt \)
  • This represents the magnitude of the velocity vector integrated over time
  • The formula works for any parametric curve in the plane
  • For straight lines, it reduces to the distance formula
  • For circles, it gives the expected circumference
  • Integration may require trigonometric identities or substitution techniques
