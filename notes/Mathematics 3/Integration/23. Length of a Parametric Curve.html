Length of a Parametric Curve

üìê Concepts

The arc length of a parametric curve \( x = f(t) \), \( y = g(t) \) for \( a \leq t \leq b \) is the total distance traveled along the curve. The formula is derived from the distance formula applied to infinitesimal segments:

\( L = \int_a^b \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2} \, dt \)

This can also be written as:

\( L = \int_a^b \sqrt{[f'(t)]^2 + [g'(t)]^2} \, dt \)
Geometric Interpretation: The integrand \( \sqrt{(dx/dt)^2 + (dy/dt)^2} \) represents the speed of the particle at time t, and integrating this speed over time gives the total distance traveled.

üîç Examples

Example 1: Straight Line

Find the arc length of the line \( x = t \), \( y = 2t \) from t=0 to t=3.

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = 1, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = 2 \)

\( L = \int_0^3 \sqrt{1^2 + 2^2} \, dt = \int_0^3 \sqrt{5} \, dt = \sqrt{5} \cdot 3 = 3\sqrt{5} \)

Example 2: Semicircle

Find the arc length of \( x = 2\cos t \), \( y = 2\sin t \) from t=0 to t=œÄ (upper semicircle).

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = -2\sin t, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = 2\cos t \)

\( L = \int_0^\pi \sqrt{(-2\sin t)^2 + (2\cos t)^2} \, dt = \int_0^\pi \sqrt{4\sin^2 t + 4\cos^2 t} \, dt \)

\( = \int_0^\pi \sqrt{4(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t)} \, dt = \int_0^\pi \sqrt{4} \, dt = \int_0^\pi 2 \, dt = 2\pi \)

Example 3: Cycloid

Find the arc length of one arch of the cycloid \( x = t - \sin t \), \( y = 1 - \cos t \) from t=0 to t=2œÄ.

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = 1 - \cos t, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = \sin t \)

\( L = \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{(1 - \cos t)^2 + (\sin t)^2} \, dt = \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{1 - 2\cos t + \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t} \, dt \)

\( = \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{2 - 2\cos t} \, dt = \sqrt{2} \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{1 - \cos t} \, dt \)

Using \( 1 - \cos t = 2\sin^2(t/2) \):

\( L = \sqrt{2} \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{2\sin^2(t/2)} \, dt = \sqrt{2} \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{2} |\sin(t/2)| \, dt = 2 \int_0^{2\pi} |\sin(t/2)| \, dt = 8 \)

üöÄ Applications

  • Physics: Calculating distances traveled by objects moving in curved paths
  • Engineering: Determining cable lengths, belt lengths in machinery, material usage for curved components
  • Computer Graphics: Computing curve lengths for animation, pathfinding, and rendering
  • Navigation: GPS path distance calculations, flight path optimization
  • Manufacturing: Calculating material requirements for curved parts, wire lengths, pipe lengths
  • Architecture: Measuring curved building elements, arch lengths, decorative curves

Problem 1: Find the arc length of the parametric curve \( x = t^2 \), \( y = \frac{2}{3}t^{3/2} \) from t=0 to t=3.

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = 2t, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{3}{2} t^{1/2} = t^{1/2} \)

\( L = \int_0^3 \sqrt{(2t)^2 + (t^{1/2})^2} \, dt = \int_0^3 \sqrt{4t^2 + t} \, dt = \int_0^3 \sqrt{t(4t + 1)} \, dt \)

Let \( u = 4t + 1, \, du = 4 dt, \, t = \frac{u-1}{4} \)

When t=0: u=1; when t=3: u=13

\( L = \int_1^{13} \sqrt{\frac{u-1}{4} \cdot u} \cdot \frac{1}{4} du = \frac{1}{4} \int_1^{13} \sqrt{\frac{(u-1)u}{4}} du = \frac{1}{4} \int_1^{13} \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{(u^2 - u)} du \)

\( = \frac{1}{8} \int_1^{13} \sqrt{u^2 - u} du = \frac{1}{8} \int_1^{13} \sqrt{(u - 1/2)^2 - 1/4} du \)

\( = \frac{1}{8} \left[ \frac{u-1/2}{2} \sqrt{u^2 - u} + \frac{1}{4} \ln|u - 1/2 + \sqrt{u^2 - u}| \right]_1^{13} \)

This is a complex integral. The result is \( \frac{1}{8} \left[ \frac{13-0.5}{2} \sqrt{169-13} + \frac{1}{4} \ln|13-0.5 + \sqrt{169-13}| - \left( \frac{1-0.5}{2} \sqrt{1-1} + \frac{1}{4} \ln|1-0.5 + \sqrt{1-1}| \right) \right] \)

\( = \frac{1}{8} \left[ 6.25 \sqrt{156} + 0.25 \ln|12.5 + \sqrt{156}| - 0 \right] \approx \frac{1}{8} (6.25 \times 12.49 + 0.25 \times 4.03) \approx \frac{1}{8} (78.06 + 1.01) \approx 9.89 \)
Problem 2: Find the arc length of the curve \( x = \cos^3 \theta \), \( y = \sin^3 \theta \) (astroid) from Œ∏=0 to Œ∏=œÄ/2.

\( \frac{dx}{d\theta} = 3\cos^2 \theta (-\sin \theta) = -3\cos^2 \theta \sin \theta \)
\( \frac{dy}{d\theta} = 3\sin^2 \theta \cos \theta \)

\( L = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sqrt{(-3\cos^2 \theta \sin \theta)^2 + (3\sin^2 \theta \cos \theta)^2} \, d\theta \)

\( = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sqrt{9\cos^4 \theta \sin^2 \theta + 9\sin^4 \theta \cos^2 \theta} \, d\theta = \int_0^{\pi/2} 3|\cos \theta \sin \theta| \sqrt{\cos^2 \theta + \sin^2 \theta} \, d\theta \)

\( = \int_0^{\pi/2} 3|\cos \theta \sin \theta| \cdot 1 \, d\theta = 3 \int_0^{\pi/2} \cos \theta \sin \theta \, d\theta \) (since both ‚â• 0 in this interval)

\( = 3 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin 2\theta \, d\theta = \frac{3}{2} \left[ -\frac{1}{2} \cos 2\theta \right]_0^{\pi/2} = -\frac{3}{4} [\cos 2\theta]_0^{\pi/2} \)

\( = -\frac{3}{4} (\cos \pi - \cos 0) = -\frac{3}{4} (-1 - 1) = -\frac{3}{4} (-2) = \frac{6}{4} = \frac{3}{2} \)
Problem 3: Find the arc length of the parametric curve \( x = e^t \cos t \), \( y = e^t \sin t \) from t=0 to t=1.

\( \frac{dx}{dt} = e^t \cos t - e^t \sin t = e^t (\cos t - \sin t) \)
\( \frac{dy}{dt} = e^t \sin t + e^t \cos t = e^t (\sin t + \cos t) \)

\( L = \int_0^1 \sqrt{[e^t (\cos t - \sin t)]^2 + [e^t (\sin t + \cos t)]^2} \, dt \)

\( = \int_0^1 \sqrt{e^{2t} [(\cos t - \sin t)^2 + (\sin t + \cos t)^2]} \, dt \)

\( = \int_0^1 \sqrt{e^{2t} [(\cos^2 t - 2\cos t \sin t + \sin^2 t) + (\sin^2 t + 2\sin t \cos t + \cos^2 t)]} \, dt \)

\( = \int_0^1 \sqrt{e^{2t} [2\cos^2 t + 2\sin^2 t]} \, dt = \int_0^1 \sqrt{e^{2t} \cdot 2} \, dt = \int_0^1 e^t \sqrt{2} \, dt \)

\( = \sqrt{2} [e^t]_0^1 = \sqrt{2} (e - 1) \)

üéØ Key Takeaways

  • Arc length formula: \( L = \int_a^b \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2} \, dt \)
  • The integrand represents the speed of the parametric particle
  • For straight lines, the formula reduces to the distance formula
  • For circles, it gives the expected arc length
  • The formula works for any smooth parametric curve
  • Integration often requires trigonometric identities or substitution techniques
  • The result is always positive, representing total distance along the curve
