Definite Integral: Integration by Parts

Integration by Parts for Definite Integrals

Integration by parts is a powerful technique for evaluating definite integrals of products of functions. It transforms the integral of a product into simpler integrals by applying the product rule in reverse. This method is particularly useful when one function in the product is easily integrable while the other becomes simpler after differentiation.

Why Integration by Parts Matters:

This technique allows us to evaluate integrals that cannot be solved using basic integration rules, such as integrals involving polynomials multiplied by trigonometric functions or exponential functions.

The Integration by Parts Formula

Definite Integration by Parts:

If u = u(x) and dv = v'(x) dx are differentiable functions, then:

\( \int_a^b u \, dv = [u v]_a^b - \int_a^b v \, du \)

Expanded form:

\( \int_a^b u(x) v'(x) \, dx = [u(x) v(x)]_a^b - \int_a^b v(x) u'(x) \, dx \)

Derivation

The formula comes from the product rule of differentiation:

\( \frac{d}{dx} [u(x) v(x)] = u'(x) v(x) + u(x) v'(x) \)

Rearranging: \( u(x) v'(x) = \frac{d}{dx} [u(x) v(x)] - u'(x) v(x) \)

Integrate both sides from a to b:

\( \int_a^b u(x) v'(x) \, dx = [u(x) v(x)]_a^b - \int_a^b u'(x) v(x) \, dx \)

Choosing u and dv

ILATE Priority (when applicable):
  • I: Inverse trigonometric functions
  • L: Logarithmic functions
  • A: Algebraic functions
  • T: Trigonometric functions
  • E: Exponential functions

Choose u to be the function that becomes simpler when differentiated, and dv to be the function that becomes simpler when integrated.

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Identify the product: Express the integrand as a product u dv
  2. Choose u and dv: Select u (to differentiate) and dv (to integrate)
  3. Compute du and v: Differentiate u to get du, integrate dv to get v
  4. Apply the formula: Substitute into the integration by parts formula
  5. Evaluate the boundary term: Compute [uv]_a^b
  6. Integrate the remaining integral: Evaluate ∫ v du
  7. Combine results: Add the boundary term and the integral

Examples

Example 1: Polynomial × Exponential

Evaluate \( \int_0^1 x e^x \, dx \)

Solution:

Let u = x, dv = e^x dx

Then du = dx, v = e^x

\( \int_0^1 x e^x \, dx = [x e^x]_0^1 - \int_0^1 e^x \, dx = (1 \cdot e^1 - 0 \cdot e^0) - [e^x]_0^1 = (e - 0) - (e^1 - e^0) = e - (e - 1) = 1 \)

Example 2: Trigonometric × Polynomial

Evaluate \( \int_0^{\pi/2} x \sin x \, dx \)

Solution:

Let u = x, dv = sin x dx

Then du = dx, v = -cos x

\( \int_0^{\pi/2} x \sin x \, dx = [x (-cos x)]_0^{\pi/2} - \int_0^{\pi/2} (-cos x) \, dx = \left( \frac{\pi}{2} (-cos \frac{\pi}{2}) - 0 \cdot (-cos 0) \right) - \int_0^{\pi/2} -cos x \, dx \)

= \( \left( \frac{\pi}{2} (0) - 0 \right) + \int_0^{\pi/2} cos x \, dx = 0 + [\sin x]_0^{\pi/2} = \sin \frac{\pi}{2} - \sin 0 = 1 - 0 = 1 \)

Example 3: Logarithmic Function

Evaluate \( \int_1^e x \ln x \, dx \)

Solution:

Let u = ln x, dv = x dx

Then du = (1/x) dx, v = (1/2) x²

\( \int_1^e x \ln x \, dx = \left[ \ln x \cdot \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right]_1^e - \int_1^e \frac{1}{2} x^2 \cdot \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \left( \ln e \cdot \frac{1}{2} e^2 - \ln 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2} (1)^2 \right) - \int_1^e \frac{1}{2} x \, dx \)

= \( \left( 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2} e^2 - 0 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \right) - \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right]_1^e = \frac{1}{2} e^2 - \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{2} e^2 - \frac{1}{2} (1)^2 \right) = \frac{1}{2} e^2 - \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{2} e^2 - \frac{1}{2} \right) \)

= \( \frac{1}{2} e^2 - \frac{1}{4} e^2 + \frac{1}{4} = \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{4} \right) e^2 + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{4} e^2 + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{4} (e^2 + 1) \)

Practice Problems

Problem 1

Evaluate \( \int_0^1 x^2 e^x \, dx \)

Solution:

Let u = x², dv = e^x dx

Then du = 2x dx, v = e^x

\( \int_0^1 x^2 e^x \, dx = [x^2 e^x]_0^1 - \int_0^1 2x e^x \, dx = (1^2 e^1 - 0^2 e^0) - 2 \int_0^1 x e^x \, dx \)

Now integrate by parts again: Let u = x, dv = e^x dx for ∫ x e^x dx

u = x, du = dx, dv = e^x dx, v = e^x

\( \int_0^1 x e^x \, dx = [x e^x]_0^1 - \int_0^1 e^x \, dx = (1 \cdot e - 0 \cdot 1) - [e^x]_0^1 = e - (e - 1) = 1 \)

Therefore: e - 2(1) = e - 2

Problem 2

Evaluate \( \int_0^{\pi/4} x \cos x \, dx \)

Solution:

Let u = x, dv = cos x dx

Then du = dx, v = sin x

\( \int_0^{\pi/4} x \cos x \, dx = [x \sin x]_0^{\pi/4} - \int_0^{\pi/4} \sin x \, dx = \left( \frac{\pi}{4} \sin \frac{\pi}{4} - 0 \cdot \sin 0 \right) - [-\cos x]_0^{\pi/4} \)

= \( \frac{\pi}{4} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} - \left( -\cos \frac{\pi}{4} - (-\cos 0) \right) = \frac{\pi \sqrt{2}}{8} - \left( -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} + 1 \right) = \frac{\pi \sqrt{2}}{8} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} - 1 \)

Problem 3

Evaluate \( \int_1^2 x \ln x \, dx \)

Solution:

Let u = ln x, dv = x dx

Then du = (1/x) dx, v = (1/2) x²

\( \int_1^2 x \ln x \, dx = \left[ \ln x \cdot \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right]_1^2 - \int_1^2 \frac{1}{2} x^2 \cdot \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \left( \ln 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} (2)^2 - \ln 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2} (1)^2 \right) - \int_1^2 \frac{1}{2} x \, dx \)

= \( \left( \ln 2 \cdot 2 - 0 \right) - \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right]_1^2 = 2 \ln 2 - \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{2} (4) - \frac{1}{2} (1) \right) = 2 \ln 2 - \frac{1}{2} \left( 2 - \frac{1}{2} \right) = 2 \ln 2 - \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{3}{2} = 2 \ln 2 - \frac{3}{4} \)

Key Takeaways

  • Integration by parts: ∫ u dv = uv - ∫ v du
  • Choose u to be differentiated, dv to be integrated
  • Use ILATE as a guide for choosing u and dv
  • Apply the formula with definite limits: [uv]_a^b - ∫ v du
  • May need to apply integration by parts multiple times
  • Useful for products of polynomials with exponentials, logs, or trig functions
  • Always evaluate the boundary term [uv]_a^b
  • The remaining integral should be simpler than the original
