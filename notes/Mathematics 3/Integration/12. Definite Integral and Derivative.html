Definite Integral and Derivative

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus establishes the deep connection between differentiation and integration, showing that these two operations are essentially inverses of each other. This theorem is one of the most important results in calculus and forms the foundation for much of mathematical analysis.

Why This Theorem Matters:

The Fundamental Theorem bridges the gap between the geometric concept of area (definite integrals) and the analytic concept of instantaneous rate of change (derivatives), providing a powerful computational tool.

Part 1: Derivative of a Definite Integral

Theorem (FTC Part 1): If \( f \) is continuous on [a, b] and x ∈ [a, b], then:

\( \frac{d}{dx} \int_a^x f(t) \, dt = f(x) \)

Interpretation:

The derivative of the area function (with respect to its upper limit) equals the value of the integrand at that point.

Proof Sketch

Let \( F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt \). By the definition of derivative:

\( F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{F(x+h) - F(x)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt \)

By the Mean Value Theorem for integrals, there exists c ∈ [x, x+h] such that:

\( \frac{1}{h} \int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt = f(c) \)

As h → 0, c → x, so F'(x) = f(x).

Part 2: Integral of a Derivative

Theorem (FTC Part 2): If F is an antiderivative of f on [a, b], then:

\( \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) \)

Interpretation:

The definite integral of a function equals the difference of its antiderivative evaluated at the limits.

Proof Sketch

Let F be an antiderivative of f. Consider the function:

\( G(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt - (F(x) - F(a)) \)

By FTC Part 1, G'(x) = f(x) - f(x) = 0, so G(x) = C (constant).

When x = a: G(a) = 0 - (F(a) - F(a)) = 0, so C = 0.

Therefore: \( \int_a^x f(t) \, dt = F(x) - F(a) \)

Setting x = b gives the result.

Generalizations

Variable Lower Limit

If both limits are variable: \( \frac{d}{dx} \int_{g(x)}^{h(x)} f(t) \, dt = f(h(x)) \cdot h'(x) - f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \)

This follows from the chain rule and FTC Part 1.

Leibniz Rule

For integrals with parameters: \( \frac{d}{d\alpha} \int_a^b f(x, \alpha) \, dx = \int_a^b \frac{\partial f}{\partial \alpha}(x, \alpha) \, dx \)

This allows differentiation under the integral sign.

Examples

Example 1: FTC Part 1

Find \( \frac{d}{dx} \int_1^x \sqrt{t^2 + 1} \, dt \)

Solution:

By FTC Part 1: \( \frac{d}{dx} \int_1^x \sqrt{t^2 + 1} \, dt = \sqrt{x^2 + 1} \)

Example 2: FTC Part 2

Evaluate \( \int_0^1 e^x \, dx \)

Solution:

F(x) = e^x is an antiderivative of e^x.

\( \int_0^1 e^x \, dx = [e^x]_0^1 = e^1 - e^0 = e - 1 \)

Example 3: Variable Limits

Find \( \frac{d}{dx} \int_{x^2}^{x^3} \sin t \, dt \)

Solution:

Using the generalization: \( \frac{d}{dx} \int_{x^2}^{x^3} \sin t \, dt = \sin(x^3) \cdot 3x^2 - \sin(x^2) \cdot 2x \)

Practice Problems

Problem 1

Find \( \frac{d}{dx} \int_2^x \frac{1}{t} \, dt \)

Solution:

By FTC Part 1: \( \frac{d}{dx} \int_2^x \frac{1}{t} \, dt = \frac{1}{x} \)

Problem 2

Evaluate \( \int_1^4 \sqrt{x} \, dx \)

Solution:

F(x) = ∫√x dx = (2/3)x^(3/2)

\( \int_1^4 \sqrt{x} \, dx = \left[ \frac{2}{3} x^{3/2} \right]_1^4 = \frac{2}{3} (8) - \frac{2}{3} (1) = \frac{16}{3} - \frac{2}{3} = \frac{14}{3} \)

Problem 3

Find \( \frac{d}{dx} \int_{x^2}^{x} \cos t \, dt \)

Solution:

Using the generalization: \( \frac{d}{dx} \int_{x^2}^{x} \cos t \, dt = \cos(x) \cdot 1 - \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x = \cos x - 2x \cos(x^2) \)

Problem 4

Evaluate \( \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin x \, dx \)

Solution:

F(x) = -cos x

\( \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin x \, dx = [-\cos x]_0^{\pi/2} = -\cos(\pi/2) - (-\cos 0) = -0 - (-1) = 1 \)

Key Takeaways

  • FTC Part 1: d/dx ∫_a^x f(t) dt = f(x)
  • FTC Part 2: ∫_a^b f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a) where F' = f
  • Integration and differentiation are inverse operations
  • The theorem connects geometric area with analytic derivatives
  • Variable limits require the chain rule generalization
  • Leibniz rule allows differentiation under the integral sign
  • FTC provides the theoretical foundation for integral evaluation
  • Both parts are essential for understanding the full relationship
