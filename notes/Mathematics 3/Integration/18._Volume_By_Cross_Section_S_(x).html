Volume by Cross Sections

The method of cross sections is a powerful technique for finding volumes of solids that are difficult to approach with other methods. Instead of slicing the solid parallel to a coordinate plane, we slice it perpendicular to one axis and find the area of each cross section.

The Method:

Imagine slicing a solid with planes perpendicular to the x-axis. If we can find the area S(x) of the cross section at each point x, then the volume is the integral of S(x) from a to b.

The Fundamental Formula

Volume Formula: If S(x) is the area of the cross section at x (perpendicular to the x-axis), then:

\( V = \int_a^b S(x) \, dx \)

Geometric Interpretation:

Each cross section contributes an area S(x), and over a small interval dx, the volume element is S(x) dx.

The total volume is the sum of all these volume elements.

Requirements

  • The solid must lie between x = a and x = b
  • S(x) must be integrable on [a, b]
  • S(x) ≥ 0 for all x in [a, b]
  • The cross sections must be perpendicular to the x-axis

Common Cross Section Shapes

Square Cross Sections

If cross sections are squares with side length s(x):

\( S(x) = [s(x)]^2 \)

\( V = \int_a^b [s(x)]^2 \, dx \)

Circular Cross Sections

If cross sections are circles with radius r(x):

\( S(x) = \pi [r(x)]^2 \)

\( V = \int_a^b \pi [r(x)]^2 \, dx \)

Rectangular Cross Sections

If cross sections are rectangles with width w(x) and height h(x):

\( S(x) = w(x) \cdot h(x) \)

\( V = \int_a^b w(x) \cdot h(x) \, dx \)

Triangular Cross Sections

If cross sections are equilateral triangles with side length s(x):

\( S(x) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} [s(x)]^2 \)

\( V = \int_a^b \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} [s(x)]^2 \, dx \)

Examples

Example 1: Square Cross Sections

Find the volume of a solid with square cross sections perpendicular to the x-axis where the side length is x + 1, from x = 0 to x = 3.

Solution:

S(x) = (x + 1)²

V = \( \int_0^3 (x + 1)^2 \, dx = \int_0^3 (x^2 + 2x + 1) \, dx \)

\( = \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} + x^2 + x \right]_0^3 = \left( \frac{27}{3} + 9 + 3 \right) - 0 = 9 + 9 + 3 = 21 \)

The volume is 21 cubic units.

Example 2: Circular Cross Sections

A solid has circular cross sections with radius √x from x = 1 to x = 4. Find the volume.

Solution:

S(x) = π(√x)² = πx

V = \( \int_1^4 \pi x \, dx = \pi \left[ \frac{x^2}{2} \right]_1^4 = \pi \left( \frac{16}{2} - \frac{1}{2} \right) = \pi \left(8 - 0.5\right) = \frac{15\pi}{2} \)

The volume is 15π/2 cubic units.

Example 3: Rectangular Cross Sections

Find the volume of a solid with rectangular cross sections where the width is x and the height is 2x, from x = 0 to x = 2.

Solution:

S(x) = x × 2x = 2x²

V = \( \int_0^2 2x^2 \, dx = 2 \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} \right]_0^2 = 2 \cdot \frac{8}{3} = \frac{16}{3} \)

The volume is 16/3 cubic units.

Practice Problems

Problem 1

Find the volume of a solid with square cross sections where the side length is 2x + 1, from x = 0 to x = 2.

Solution:

S(x) = (2x + 1)² = 4x² + 4x + 1

V = \( \int_0^2 (4x^2 + 4x + 1) \, dx = \left[ \frac{4x^3}{3} + 2x^2 + x \right]_0^2 \)

\( = \left( \frac{32}{3} + 8 + 2 \right) - 0 = \frac{32}{3} + 10 = \frac{32}{3} + \frac{30}{3} = \frac{62}{3} \)

Problem 2

A solid has circular cross sections with radius x/2 from x = 0 to x = 4. Find the volume.

Solution:

S(x) = π(x/2)² = π(x²/4)

V = \( \int_0^4 \frac{\pi x^2}{4} \, dx = \frac{\pi}{4} \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} \right]_0^4 = \frac{\pi}{4} \cdot \frac{64}{3} = \frac{16\pi}{3} \)

Problem 3

Find the volume of a solid with equilateral triangular cross sections where the side length is x + 1, from x = 0 to x = 2.

Solution:

S(x) = \( \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} (x + 1)^2 \)

V = \( \int_0^2 \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} (x + 1)^2 \, dx = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \int_0^2 (x^2 + 2x + 1) \, dx \)

\( = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} + x^2 + x \right]_0^2 = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \left( \frac{8}{3} + 4 + 2 \right) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \cdot \frac{22}{3} = \frac{11\sqrt{3}}{6} \)

Key Takeaways

  • Volume = ∫ S(x) dx where S(x) is cross-sectional area at x
  • Cross sections must be perpendicular to the axis of integration
  • Common shapes: squares, circles, rectangles, triangles
  • S(x) must be ≥ 0 and integrable on [a, b]
  • Each cross section contributes S(x) dx to the volume
  • Choose the axis that makes S(x) easiest to express
  • Sketch the solid and cross sections to understand the geometry
  • This method works for solids with varying cross sections
