Incenter & Angle Bisectors

Geometry • Triangle Properties

Angle Bisector Theorem

In any triangle, the three angle bisectors always intersect at a single point called the incenter. The incenter is equidistant from all three sides of the triangle.

If $I$ is the incenter of $\triangle ABC$, then:
$d(IA) = d(IB) = d(IC) = r$
where $r$ is the inradius.

Understanding the Incenter

The incenter is the center of the incircle (inscribed circle) - the largest circle that can be drawn inside the triangle, touching all three sides. The angle bisectors are the lines that divide each angle into two equal parts.

Key Properties:

  • The incenter is always located inside the triangle
  • The incenter is equidistant from all three sides
  • The incircle touches each side of the triangle at exactly one point
  • The points of tangency divide each side into segments proportional to the adjacent sides

Proof: Existence of Incenter

Step 1: Angle Bisectors

Consider $\triangle ABC$. Draw the angle bisectors from each vertex to the opposite side. Each angle bisector divides the angle into two equal parts.

Step 2: Intersection Point

The angle bisectors from vertices $A$ and $B$ intersect at point $I$. We need to show that the angle bisector from $C$ also passes through $I$.

Step 3: Distance to Sides

Draw perpendiculars from point $I$ to each side. Since $I$ lies on the angle bisectors, and the angle bisectors are equidistant from the sides of equal angles, $I$ is equidistant from all three sides.

Step 4: Incircle Construction

The circle with center $I$ and radius equal to the distance from $I$ to any side will be tangent to all three sides, forming the incircle.

Example 1: Finding Incenter Coordinates

Find the incenter of $\triangle ABC$ with vertices $A(0,0)$, $B(6,0)$, and $C(3,4)$.

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate side lengths:

  • $a = BC = \sqrt{(6-3)^2 + (0-4)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = 5$
  • $b = AC = \sqrt{(3-0)^2 + (4-0)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = 5$
  • $c = AB = \sqrt{(6-0)^2 + (0-0)^2} = 6$

Step 2: Calculate semiperimeter $s = (a+b+c)/2 = (5+5+6)/2 = 8$

Step 3: Calculate inradius $r = \sqrt{\frac{(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)}{s}} = \sqrt{\frac{(8-5)(8-5)(8-6)}{8}} = \sqrt{\frac{3\cdot3\cdot2}{8}} = \sqrt{\frac{18}{8}} = \sqrt{\frac{9}{4}} = \frac{3}{2} = 1.5$

Step 4: Find incenter coordinates:

$I_x = \frac{aA_x + bB_x + cC_x}{a+b+c} = \frac{5\cdot0 + 5\cdot6 + 6\cdot3}{5+5+6} = \frac{0 + 30 + 18}{16} = \frac{48}{16} = 3$

$I_y = \frac{aA_y + bB_y + cC_y}{a+b+c} = \frac{5\cdot0 + 5\cdot0 + 6\cdot4}{16} = \frac{24}{16} = 1.5$

Incenter: $I(3, 1.5)$

Applications of Incenter

Triangle Incircle

The incircle represents the largest circle that can fit inside any triangle. This is important in geometry optimization problems and computer graphics for collision detection.

Angle Bisectors

Angle bisectors have applications in navigation and surveying, where they help determine the center of angles and create balanced divisions of space.

Engineering Applications

In civil engineering, the concept of incenter helps in designing drainage systems and determining optimal locations for utilities within triangular plots of land.

Practice Problems

1. Find the inradius of $\triangle ABC$ with sides 7, 8, and 9 units.

Semiperimeter: $s = (7+8+9)/2 = 12$

Inradius: $r = \sqrt{\frac{(12-7)(12-8)(12-9)}{12}} = \sqrt{\frac{5\cdot4\cdot3}{12}} = \sqrt{\frac{60}{12}} = \sqrt{5} \approx 2.236$

2. In $\triangle ABC$, the incenter is at $(2,3)$ and the inradius is 1.5. Find the area of the triangle.

The area $K$ of a triangle is related to the inradius by $K = r \cdot s$, where $s$ is the semiperimeter.

3. In $\triangle ABC$, the incircle touches $BC$ at $D$. If $AB = 5$, $AC = 4$, and $BD = 2$, find $DC$.

By the property of points of tangency: $AB = 5$, so $AF + FB = 5$.
$BD = 2$, so $FB = 2$, thus $AF = 5 - 2 = 3$.
$DC = s - c$, but we need to find $s$ and $c$.

This requires more information about the triangle. We need either another tangent length or the angle at A.

4. Find the incenter of an equilateral triangle with side length 6 units.

In an equilateral triangle, the incenter coincides with the centroid and circumcenter.

Height: $h = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \times 6 = 3\sqrt{3}$

Inradius: $r = \frac{h}{3} = \sqrt{3} \approx 1.732$

Incenter: Located at a distance of $r$ from each side, at the centroid.

5. Prove that the angle bisectors of a triangle are concurrent.

Consider $\triangle ABC$. Draw angle bisectors from $A$ and $B$, intersecting at $I$.
Show that $\angle AIC = \angle BIC$.
In $\triangle AIC$ and $\triangle BIC$, we have $\angle IAC = \angle IBC$ (given), and $\angle ICA = \angle ICB$ (since $IC$ is common).
Thus $\triangle AIC \cong \triangle BIC$ by ASA, so $\angle AIC = \angle BIC$.
Therefore, $I$ lies on the angle bisector from $C$.

Key Takeaways

  • The incenter is the intersection point of the three angle bisectors of a triangle.
  • The incenter is equidistant from all three sides of the triangle.
  • The incircle is tangent to all three sides and is the largest circle that can fit inside the triangle.
  • The inradius $r$ can be calculated using $r = \sqrt{\frac{(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)}{s}}$ where $s$ is the semiperimeter.
  • The points of tangency divide each side into segments equal to the tangents from the adjacent vertices.
  • The incenter coordinates can be found using weighted averages: $I_x = \frac{aA_x + bB_x + cC_x}{a+b+c}$
  • The incenter always lies inside the triangle, regardless of the triangle type.
