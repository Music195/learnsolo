Converse of the Inscribed Angle Theorem

Converse of the Inscribed Angle Theorem

If two angles subtended by the same chord (or arc) are equal, then the points lie on the same circle.

If ∠APB = ∠AQB, then points A, B, P, Q are concyclic.

Geometric Interpretation

The converse of the inscribed angle theorem states that if you have equal angles subtended by the same chord or arc, then all four points must lie on a single circle. This theorem is crucial for proving that certain configurations of points are concyclic (lie on the same circle).

The theorem helps identify when four points form a cyclic quadrilateral or when additional points lie on a circle defined by others.

Proof of the Converse

Step 1: Assume equal angles

Assume ∠APB = ∠AQB, where both angles subtend the same chord AB.

Step 2: Apply inscribed angle theorem

By the inscribed angle theorem, ∠APB = ½∠AOB and ∠AQB = ½∠AOB, where ∠AOB is the central angle.

Step 3: Equal central angles

Since ∠APB = ∠AQB, then ½∠AOB = ½∠AOB, so ∠AOB = ∠AOB. This means both angles subtend the same arc.

Step 4: Points lie on the circle

Since both angles subtend the same arc AB, points A, B, P, Q all lie on the circle with center O and radius OA.

Step 5: Conclusion

Therefore, the four points A, B, P, Q are concyclic.

Example 1: Verifying Concyclic Points

Points A(0,0), B(4,0), P(2,3), Q(2,1) are given. Check if they are concyclic by comparing ∠APB and ∠AQB.

Calculate ∠APB:
Vectors: AB = (4,0), AP = (2,3), BP = (2-4,3-0) = (-2,3)
cos∠APB = (AB•AP)/(|AB||AP|) = (8)/(4×√13) = 8/(4√13) = 2/√13
∠APB = cos⁻¹(2/√13) ≈ 30°
Calculate ∠AQB:
Vectors: AB = (4,0), AQ = (2,1), BQ = (2-4,1-0) = (-2,1)
cos∠AQB = (AB•AQ)/(|AB||AQ|) = (8)/(4×√5) = 8/(4√5) = 2/√5
∠AQB = cos⁻¹(2/√5) ≈ 30°
Conclusion: ∠APB = ∠AQB ≈ 30°, so points are concyclic.

Example 2: Finding the Circle

Given ∠APB = ∠AQB = 45° and chord AB = 6 cm, find the radius of the circle.

Given: ∠APB = ∠AQB = 45°, AB = 6 cm
Inscribed angle theorem: ∠APB = ½∠AOB = 45°, so ∠AOB = 90°
Central angle: ∠AOB = 90° for arc AB
Arc length: Arc AB = rθ = r × (π/2) where θ = 90° = π/2 radians
Chord length: AB = 2r sin(θ/2) = 2r sin(45°) = 2r(√2/2) = r√2 = 6
r = 6/√2 = 3√2 cm

Answer: The radius is 3√2 cm.

Applications of the Converse

1. Cyclic Quadrilaterals

The theorem helps prove that certain quadrilaterals are cyclic (all vertices lie on a circle).

2. Circle Construction

Used to construct circles that pass through given points with specific angle conditions.

3. Geometric Proofs

Essential for proving that points lie on the same circle in complex geometric configurations.

4. Trigonometric Applications

Helps in solving trigonometric problems involving angles in circles.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: If ∠APB = ∠AQB = 60°, what is the measure of the central angle subtending arc AB?
Solution:
Inscribed angle = ½ × central angle
60° = ½ × central angle
Central angle = 120°
Answer: 120°
Problem 2: Points A, B, P, Q are such that ∠APB = ∠AQB. What can you conclude about these points?
Solution:
By the converse of the inscribed angle theorem, the points A, B, P, Q lie on the same circle.
Answer: The points are concyclic.
Problem 3: In circle O, ∠APB = 35° and ∠AQB = 35°. If AB = 8 cm, find the radius.
Solution:
Central angle ∠AOB = 2 × 35° = 70°
AB = 2r sin(θ/2) = 2r sin(35°)
8 = 2r × 0.5736
r = 8 / (2 × 0.5736) ≈ 8 / 1.1472 ≈ 7.0 cm
Answer: Approximately 7.0 cm
Problem 4: If ∠APB = ∠AQB and ∠APB = 45°, what is the measure of the intercepted arc AB?
Solution:
Inscribed angle = ½ × intercepted arc
45° = ½ × intercepted arc
Intercepted arc = 90°
Answer: 90°
Problem 5: Points P and Q are on the same side of chord AB. If ∠APB ≠ ∠AQB, what can you conclude?
Solution:
If the angles are not equal, the points do not lie on the same circle.
Answer: Points A, B, P, Q are not concyclic.

Key Takeaways

  • The converse states that equal angles subtended by the same chord imply concyclic points
  • The theorem is essential for proving that four points lie on a circle
  • It works in both directions: equal inscribed angles → same circle
  • The theorem applies to any chord and any points on the remaining parts of the circle
  • It forms the basis for many circle theorems and cyclic quadrilateral properties
  • The theorem is widely used in geometric constructions and proofs
