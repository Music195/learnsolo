Integer Division

Core Concept: Division Algorithm

The Division Algorithm (also known as the Division Theorem) states that for any integer a and any positive integer b, there exist unique integers q (quotient) and r (remainder) such that:

Division Algorithm Theorem

$a = bq + r,\quad 0 \leq r < b$

Where:

  • a is the dividend
  • b is the divisor (positive integer)
  • q is the quotient
  • r is the remainder (0 ≤ r < b)

This theorem guarantees that division with remainder is always possible and the result is unique.

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Positive Numbers

Divide 17 by 5:

17 ÷ 5 = 3 (quotient) with remainder 2
Check: 5 × 3 + 2 = 15 + 2 = 17 ✓

Example 2: Negative Dividend

Divide -17 by 5:

-17 ÷ 5 = -4 (quotient) with remainder 3
Check: 5 × (-4) + 3 = -20 + 3 = -17 ✓
Note: Remainder is always non-negative

Example 3: Exact Division

Divide 24 by 6:

24 ÷ 6 = 4 (quotient) with remainder 0
Check: 6 × 4 + 0 = 24 ✓

Applications

  • Clock Arithmetic: Used in modular arithmetic and time calculations
  • Computer Science: Essential for implementing division operations in programming
  • Number Theory: Foundation for modular arithmetic and the study of remainders
  • Cryptography: Used in RSA algorithm and other cryptographic systems
  • Calendar Calculations: Determining days of the week, leap years, etc.

Practice Problems

1

Find the quotient and remainder when 47 is divided by 8.

47 ÷ 8 = 5 (quotient) with remainder 7

Check: 8 × 5 + 7 = 40 + 7 = 47 ✓

2

Find the quotient and remainder when -23 is divided by 6.

-23 ÷ 6 = -4 (quotient) with remainder 1

Check: 6 × (-4) + 1 = -24 + 1 = -23 ✓

3

What is the remainder when 123 is divided by 13?

123 ÷ 13 = 9 (quotient) with remainder 6

Check: 13 × 9 + 6 = 117 + 6 = 123 ✓

The remainder is 6.

4

Find integers q and r such that 89 = 11q + r, where 0 ≤ r < 11.

89 ÷ 11 = 8 (quotient) with remainder 1

So q = 8, r = 1

Check: 11 × 8 + 1 = 88 + 1 = 89 ✓

Key Takeaways

  • The Division Algorithm works for any integer dividend and positive integer divisor
  • The remainder r is always non-negative and less than the divisor b
  • The quotient q can be positive, negative, or zero
  • This theorem is fundamental to modular arithmetic
  • The result is unique for each pair (a, b)
