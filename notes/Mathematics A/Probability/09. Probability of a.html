Probability of Event A

Core Concept

Probability of an event measures how likely it is for that event to occur. It is calculated as the ratio of favorable outcomes to total possible outcomes, assuming all outcomes are equally likely.

Basic Probability Formula

P(A) = $\frac{\text{Number of favorable outcomes}}{\text{Total number of possible outcomes}} = \frac{n(A)}{N}$

Where n(A) is the number of outcomes where event A occurs, and N is the total number of equally likely outcomes

Understanding the Formula

Key Components

  • P(A): Probability of event A occurring
  • n(A): Number of ways event A can happen
  • N: Total number of possible outcomes in the sample space
  • Equally likely: All outcomes must have the same chance of occurring

Probability Range

Probability values always fall between 0 and 1:

  • P(A) = 0: Impossible event (never occurs)
  • 0 < P(A) < 1: Possible event (may or may not occur)
  • P(A) = 1: Certain event (always occurs)

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Rolling a Die

What is the probability of rolling a 4 on a fair six-sided die?

Total possible outcomes: N = 6 (faces: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6)
Favorable outcomes: n(A) = 1 (only the 4)
Formula: P(A) = n(A)/N = 1/6 ≈ 0.1667 or 16.67%
This is a fair die, so all outcomes are equally likely

Example 2: Drawing Cards

What is the probability of drawing a heart from a standard deck of 52 cards?

Total cards: N = 52
Heart cards: n(A) = 13 (4 suits × 13 ranks, but only hearts)
Formula: P(A) = 13/52 = 1/4 = 0.25 or 25%
Each card is equally likely to be drawn

Example 3: Coin Toss

What is the probability of getting heads when flipping a fair coin?

Total outcomes: N = 2 (heads or tails)
Favorable outcomes: n(A) = 1 (heads)
Formula: P(A) = 1/2 = 0.5 or 50%
Coin is fair, so both outcomes equally likely

Example 4: Multiple Events

What is the probability of rolling an even number on a die?

Total outcomes: N = 6
Even numbers: n(A) = 3 (2, 4, 6)
Formula: P(A) = 3/6 = 1/2 = 0.5 or 50%
Event A includes multiple favorable outcomes

Applications

  • Gaming: Odds in dice games, card games, and lotteries
  • Weather: Probability of rain, snow, or sunshine
  • Sports: Chance of winning, scoring, or specific outcomes
  • Quality Control: Probability of defective products
  • Medical: Success rates of treatments or disease occurrence
  • Finance: Risk assessment and investment probabilities
  • Insurance: Calculating premiums based on risk probabilities

Practice Problems

1

What is the probability of drawing a spade from a standard deck of 52 cards?

Total cards: N = 52

Spade cards: n(A) = 13

P(A) = 13/52 = 1/4 = 0.25 or 25%

2

A bag contains 5 red balls and 3 blue balls. What is the probability of drawing a red ball?

Total balls: N = 5 + 3 = 8

Red balls: n(A) = 5

P(A) = 5/8 = 0.625 or 62.5%

3

What is the probability of rolling a number greater than 4 on a fair six-sided die?

Total outcomes: N = 6

Numbers > 4: n(A) = 2 (5 and 6)

P(A) = 2/6 = 1/3 ≈ 0.333 or 33.3%

4

In a class of 30 students, 12 are girls. If one student is selected randomly, what is the probability that it is a boy?

Total students: N = 30

Girls: 12, so Boys: 30 - 12 = 18

P(Boy) = 18/30 = 3/5 = 0.6 or 60%

Key Takeaways

  • Basic formula: P(A) = n(A)/N where all outcomes are equally likely
  • Probability range: Always between 0 and 1 (or 0% to 100%)
  • Impossible event: P(A) = 0 (never happens)
  • Certain event: P(A) = 1 (always happens)
  • Equally likely: All outcomes must have the same probability
  • Multiple outcomes: Count all favorable outcomes for the event
  • Fraction/decimal/percent: Probability can be expressed in different forms
