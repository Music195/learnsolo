Multiplication Rule of Probability

Core Concept

The multiplication rule helps us find the probability that two or more events occur together. It comes in two forms: one for independent events and one for dependent events using conditional probability.

Multiplication Rule for Dependent Events

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A)

Where P(B|A) is the conditional probability of B given A

Multiplication Rule for Independent Events

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)

When events A and B are independent

Understanding Conditional Probability

Conditional Probability P(B|A)

The probability of event B occurring, given that event A has already occurred:

P(B|A) = $\frac{P(A ∩ B)}{P(A)}$

This is the probability of B in the reduced sample space where A has occurred

Why Use Conditional Probability?

  • When events are dependent (one affects the other)
  • When we have additional information about one event
  • When we're dealing with sequential events
  • When the sample space changes after the first event

Independent vs Dependent Events

Independent Events

  • Occurrence of one event doesn't affect the other
  • P(B|A) = P(B) - no change in probability
  • Use: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
  • Examples: Coin tosses, die rolls, card draws with replacement

Dependent Events

  • Occurrence of one event changes the probability of the other
  • P(B|A) ≠ P(B) - probability changes
  • Use: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A)
  • Examples: Card draws without replacement, dependent sequences

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Independent Events (Coin Tosses)

What is the probability of getting heads on the first coin and tails on the second coin?

P(Heads on first) = 1/2 = 0.5
P(Tails on second) = 1/2 = 0.5
Events are independent (one coin doesn't affect the other)
Formula: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25
Same as probability of getting HT in sequence

Example 2: Dependent Events (Cards)

What is the probability of drawing two hearts from a deck without replacement?

P(First card is heart) = 13/52 = 1/4
P(Second card is heart | First was heart) = 12/51
Events are dependent (second probability changes)
Formula: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A) = (13/52) × (12/51)
Calculation: (13/52) × (12/51) = (13 × 12) / (52 × 51) = 156/2652 = 78/1326 = 39/663 ≈ 0.0588
About 5.88% probability

Example 3: Quality Control

A factory produces items with 5% defect rate. What is the probability of finding two defective items in a row?

P(First item defective) = 0.05
P(Second item defective | First was defective) = 0.05
Assuming independent production
Formula: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) = 0.05 × 0.05 = 0.0025
0.25% probability of two defects in a row

Example 4: Medical Testing

The probability of having a disease is 0.01. If you have the disease, the test is 95% accurate. What is the probability of having the disease and testing positive?

P(Disease) = 0.01
P(Positive test | Disease) = 0.95
Formula: P(D ∩ T) = P(D) × P(T|D) = 0.01 × 0.95 = 0.0095
0.95% probability of having disease and testing positive
This is different from the probability of testing positive overall

Applications

  • Card Games: Probabilities of specific card combinations
  • Quality Control: Multiple defect probabilities
  • Medical Testing: Disease and test result combinations
  • Reliability: System component failure probabilities
  • Sports: Multiple event success probabilities
  • Computer Science: Sequential operation success rates
  • Finance: Multiple investment outcome probabilities

Practice Problems

1

What is the probability of rolling a 6 on two dice? (Assume fair dice)

Independent events: P(6 on first) = 1/6, P(6 on second) = 1/6

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) = (1/6) × (1/6) = 1/36 ≈ 0.0278

2.78% probability

2

A bag has 5 red and 3 blue balls. What is the probability of drawing two red balls without replacement?

P(First red) = 5/8

P(Second red | First red) = 4/7

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A) = (5/8) × (4/7) = 20/56 = 5/14 ≈ 0.357

35.7% probability

3

The probability of rain tomorrow is 0.3. If it rains, the probability of flooding is 0.2. What is the probability of rain and flooding?

P(Rain) = 0.3

P(Flood | Rain) = 0.2

P(R ∩ F) = P(R) × P(F|R) = 0.3 × 0.2 = 0.06

6% probability

4

A student has 80% chance of passing exam A. If they pass A, they have 90% chance of passing exam B. What is the probability they pass both exams?

P(Pass A) = 0.8

P(Pass B | Pass A) = 0.9

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A) = 0.8 × 0.9 = 0.72

72% probability

Key Takeaways

  • Dependent Events: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A)
  • Independent Events: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
  • Conditional Probability: P(B|A) = P(A ∩ B)/P(A)
  • Tree Diagrams: Useful for visualizing sequential events
  • Order Matters: For dependent events, the order of multiplication matters
  • Multiple Events: Extend to three or more events using the same logic
  • Real-World: Most real-world events are dependent, not independent
