Probability of the Complement Event

Core Concept

The complement of an event A (denoted A' or $\overline{A}$) consists of all outcomes in the sample space that are not in A. The complement rule states that the probability of the complement plus the probability of the event itself equals 1.

Complement Rule

P(A') = 1 - P(A)

Where A' is the complement of event A

Understanding Complements

What is a Complement?

  • Event A: What we want to happen
  • Complement A': Everything else that could happen instead
  • Sample Space S: All possible outcomes = A ∪ A'
  • Key Relationship: A and A' are mutually exclusive and exhaustive

Why the Rule Works

The sample space S can be divided into two mutually exclusive parts: A and A'. Since S is certain to occur:

P(S) = P(A ∪ A') = P(A) + P(A') = 1

Therefore: P(A') = 1 - P(A)

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Coin Toss

A fair coin is tossed. What is the probability of not getting heads?

P(Heads) = 1/2 = 0.5
Complement: Not getting heads = Getting tails
Formula: P(A') = 1 - P(A) = 1 - 0.5 = 0.5
Direct calculation: P(Tails) = 1/2 = 0.5
Same result using either method

Example 2: Die Roll

A fair six-sided die is rolled. What is the probability of not rolling a 6?

P(6) = 1/6 ≈ 0.1667
Complement: Not rolling a 6 = Rolling 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5
Formula: P(A') = 1 - P(A) = 1 - 1/6 = 5/6 ≈ 0.8333
Direct count: 5 favorable outcomes out of 6 total

Example 3: Card Drawing

A card is drawn from a standard deck. What is the probability of not drawing a heart?

P(Heart) = 13/52 = 1/4 = 0.25
Complement: Not drawing a heart = Drawing clubs, diamonds, or spades
Formula: P(A') = 1 - P(A) = 1 - 0.25 = 0.75
Direct count: 39 non-heart cards out of 52 total
39/52 = 0.75 (same result)

Example 4: Multiple Events

In a class, the probability that a student passes an exam is 0.8. What is the probability that a student fails?

P(Pass) = 0.8
Complement: Failing the exam
Formula: P(Fail) = 1 - P(Pass) = 1 - 0.8 = 0.2
20% of students fail the exam

Applications

  • Quality Control: Probability of products not being defective
  • Medical Testing: False negative rates and test reliability
  • Insurance: Probability of claims not being filed
  • Computer Systems: Probability of system not failing
  • Sports: Probability of not scoring or not winning
  • Weather: Probability of no rain or no snow
  • Finance: Probability of investments not losing money

Practice Problems

1

If the probability of rain tomorrow is 0.3, what is the probability of no rain?

P(Rain) = 0.3

P(No Rain) = 1 - P(Rain) = 1 - 0.3 = 0.7

70% chance of no rain tomorrow

2

A student has a 75% chance of passing an exam. What is the probability of failing?

P(Pass) = 0.75

P(Fail) = 1 - P(Pass) = 1 - 0.75 = 0.25

25% chance of failing the exam

3

The probability of a machine breaking down in a day is 0.05. What is the probability it works all day?

P(Break down) = 0.05

P(Works) = 1 - P(Break down) = 1 - 0.05 = 0.95

95% chance the machine works all day

4

In a lottery, your chance of winning is 1/1000000. What is the probability of not winning?

P(Win) = 1/1000000 = 0.000001

P(Not Win) = 1 - P(Win) = 1 - 0.000001 = 0.999999

99.9999% chance of not winning

Key Takeaways

  • Complement Rule: P(A') = 1 - P(A) for any event A
  • Complement A': All outcomes not in event A
  • Mutually Exclusive: A and A' have no outcomes in common
  • Exhaustive: A ∪ A' = S (sample space)
  • Practical Use: Often easier to calculate P(A') than P(A)
  • Range: If P(A) = p, then P(A') = 1 - p
  • Double Complement: (A')' = A (complement of complement is original)
