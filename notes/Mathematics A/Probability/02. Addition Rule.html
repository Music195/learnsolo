Addition Rule for Counting

Core Concept

The Addition Rule is a fundamental principle in counting that helps us determine the total number of ways to choose items from different groups when the groups are mutually exclusive (no overlap).

Basic Addition Rule

If A and B are mutually exclusive events, then:
$n(A \cup B) = n(A) + n(B)$

Types of Addition Rules

Mutually Exclusive Events

When events cannot occur simultaneously. The total count is simply the sum of individual counts.

$n(A \cup B) = n(A) + n(B)$

General Addition Rule

When events may overlap. We must subtract the intersection to avoid double-counting.

$n(A \cup B) = n(A) + n(B) - n(A \cap B)$

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Mutually Exclusive Events

A restaurant offers 3 types of appetizers and 4 types of desserts. A customer can choose either an appetizer OR a dessert, but not both. How many choices does the customer have?

n(Appetizers) = 3
n(Desserts) = 4
n(Appetizers ∪ Desserts) = 3 + 4 = 7

Example 2: Overlapping Events (General Rule)

In a class of 30 students, 18 play soccer, 12 play basketball, and 5 play both sports. How many students play either soccer or basketball?

n(Soccer) = 18
n(Basketball) = 12
n(Soccer ∩ Basketball) = 5
n(Soccer ∪ Basketball) = 18 + 12 - 5 = 25

Applications

  • Menu Planning: Counting different meal combinations
  • Survey Analysis: Counting respondents who chose different options
  • Inventory Management: Counting items in different categories
  • Event Planning: Counting participants in different activities
  • Database Queries: Counting records matching different criteria

Practice Problems

1

A deck of cards has 52 cards: 26 red and 26 black. If you draw one card, what is the probability of getting a red OR a black card?

This is a mutually exclusive case since a card cannot be both red and black.

$P(\text{Red} \cup \text{Black}) = P(\text{Red}) + P(\text{Black}) = \frac{26}{52} + \frac{26}{52} = \frac{52}{52} = 1$

The probability is 1 (certain), which makes sense since every card is either red or black.

2

In a group of 100 people, 60 like chocolate, 50 like vanilla, and 20 like both. How many people like chocolate OR vanilla?

This requires the general addition rule since there are people who like both.

$n(\text{Chocolate} \cup \text{Vanilla}) = 60 + 50 - 20 = 90$

90 people like chocolate or vanilla (or both).

3

A store sells shirts in 3 sizes (S, M, L) and 4 colors (red, blue, green, yellow). How many different shirt options are there if each combination of size and color is available?

This is actually a multiplication rule problem, not addition rule. The total number of options is $3 \times 4 = 12$.

The addition rule would apply if we were counting shirts that are either size S OR red, but that's not what the problem asks.

Key Takeaways

  • The basic addition rule applies only to mutually exclusive events: $n(A \cup B) = n(A) + n(B)$
  • The general addition rule accounts for overlap: $n(A \cup B) = n(A) + n(B) - n(A \cap B)$
  • Always check if events can occur simultaneously before applying the rule
  • The rule prevents double-counting when events overlap
  • Common applications include counting combinations and calculating probabilities
