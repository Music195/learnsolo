Probability of Independent Trials

Core Concept

Independent events are events where the occurrence of one event does not affect the probability of the other event occurring. When events are independent, we can multiply their probabilities to find the probability that both occur.

Multiplication Rule for Independent Events

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)

For two independent events A and B

General Multiplication Rule

P(A₁ ∩ A₂ ∩ ... ∩ Aₙ) = P(A₁) × P(A₂) × ... × P(Aₙ)

For n independent events A₁ through Aₙ

What is Independence?

Definition of Independent Events

Two events A and B are independent if:

  • The occurrence of A does not affect the probability of B
  • The occurrence of B does not affect the probability of A
  • P(A|B) = P(A) and P(B|A) = P(B)
  • Knowledge of one event gives no information about the other

Examples of Independent Events

  • Coin tosses: Each toss is independent of previous tosses
  • Die rolls: Each roll is independent of previous rolls
  • Card draws with replacement: Each draw is independent
  • Weather: Today's weather is independent of yesterday's

Examples of Dependent Events

  • Card draws without replacement: Second draw depends on first
  • Weather sequences: Tomorrow's weather may depend on today's
  • Manufacturing defects: One defect may indicate quality issues

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Two Coin Tosses

What is the probability of getting two heads when tossing two fair coins?

P(Heads on first coin) = 1/2
P(Heads on second coin) = 1/2
Events are independent (one coin doesn't affect the other)
Formula: P(HH) = P(H₁) × P(H₂) = (1/2) × (1/2) = 1/4 = 0.25
Total possible outcomes: HH, HT, TH, TT (4 equally likely outcomes)
Only 1 favorable outcome: HH

Example 2: Multiple Die Rolls

What is the probability of rolling a 6 on three consecutive rolls of a fair die?

P(6 on one die) = 1/6
Three independent rolls
Formula: P(6,6,6) = P(6) × P(6) × P(6) = (1/6)³ = 1/216 ≈ 0.00463
Very unlikely: only 1 chance in 216

Example 3: Card Draws with Replacement

A card is drawn, replaced, and drawn again. What is the probability of drawing two hearts?

P(Heart on first draw) = 13/52 = 1/4
Card is replaced, so second draw is independent
P(Heart on second draw) = 13/52 = 1/4
Formula: P(H,H) = P(H₁) × P(H₂) = (1/4) × (1/4) = 1/16 = 0.0625
With replacement: events are independent

Example 4: Quality Control

A factory produces items with a 2% defect rate. What is the probability that 3 items in a row are defective?

P(Defective) = 0.02
Assuming independent production
Formula: P(D,D,D) = P(D) × P(D) × P(D) = 0.02³ = 0.000008
Very rare: 8 in 1,000,000 items

Applications

  • Gambling: Multiple coin tosses, dice rolls, or card games
  • Quality Control: Multiple product inspections
  • Computer Science: Independent system failures or successes
  • Medicine: Multiple independent tests or trials
  • Weather: Independent daily weather events
  • Sports: Independent game outcomes or player performances
  • Finance: Independent investment returns

Practice Problems

1

What is the probability of flipping three heads in a row with a fair coin?

P(Heads) = 1/2 for each flip

Three independent flips

P(HHH) = (1/2) × (1/2) × (1/2) = 1/8 = 0.125

2

A die is rolled twice. What is the probability of getting a 4 both times?

P(4 on one roll) = 1/6

Two independent rolls

P(4,4) = (1/6) × (1/6) = 1/36 ≈ 0.0278

3

The probability of rain each day is 0.3. What is the probability of rain for 2 consecutive days?

P(Rain each day) = 0.3

Assuming independent days

P(Rain 2 days) = 0.3 × 0.3 = 0.09

4

A multiple choice test has 4 questions, each with 5 options. A student guesses randomly. What is the probability of getting all 4 correct?

P(Correct guess per question) = 1/5 = 0.2

Four independent guesses

P(All correct) = (1/5)⁴ = 1/625 = 0.0016

Key Takeaways

  • Independence: P(A|B) = P(A) and P(B|A) = P(B)
  • Multiplication Rule: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) for independent events
  • Multiple Events: P(A₁ ∩ A₂ ∩ ... ∩ Aₙ) = P(A₁) × P(A₂) × ... × P(Aₙ)
  • Common Examples: Coin tosses, die rolls, card draws with replacement
  • Not Independent: Card draws without replacement, dependent sequences
  • Tree Diagrams: Useful for visualizing independent event probabilities
  • Counterintuitive: Multiple independent rare events can be extremely unlikely
