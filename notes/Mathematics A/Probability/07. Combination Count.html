Counting Combinations

Core Concept

A combination is a selection of items from a set where the order doesn't matter. Unlike permutations, combinations focus on which items are chosen, not the arrangement order.

Combination Formula

$^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} = \frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r(r-1)\cdots(1)}$

Where n is the total number of items and r is the number of items to choose

Combinations vs Permutations

Key Differences

Aspect Combinations Permutations
Order matters? No Yes
Formula $^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$ $^nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$
Example (3 from 5) 10 ways 60 ways
Use case Selecting committee members Arranging race winners

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Committee Selection

From 8 people, how many ways can we choose a committee of 3?

n = 8 people total
r = 3 people to choose
Formula: $^8C_3 = \frac{8!}{3!(8-3)!} = \frac{40320}{6 × 120} = \frac{40320}{720} = 56$
Alternative: $^8C_3 = \frac{8 × 7 × 6}{3 × 2 × 1} = \frac{336}{6} = 56$

Example 2: Lottery Odds

In a lottery where you pick 6 numbers from 49, how many possible combinations?

n = 49 numbers available
r = 6 numbers to pick
Formula: $^{49}C_6 = \frac{49!}{6!(49-6)!} = \frac{49!}{6! × 43!}$
Calculation: $^{49}C_6 = \frac{49 × 48 × 47 × 46 × 45 × 44}{6 × 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1} = \frac{10,068,347,520}{720} = 13,983,816$

Example 3: Pizza Toppings

From 8 available toppings, how many ways can you choose 4 for your pizza?

n = 8 toppings available
r = 4 toppings to choose
Formula: $^8C_4 = \frac{8!}{4!(8-4)!} = \frac{40320}{24 × 24} = \frac{40320}{576} = 70$
Note: $^8C_4 = ^8C_{8-4} = ^8C_4$, so we could also calculate $^8C_4$

Applications

  • Lotteries: Calculating odds of winning number combinations
  • Committee Selection: Choosing members for groups or teams
  • Menu Planning: Selecting items from available options
  • Research: Forming experimental groups from participants
  • Quality Control: Sampling items for testing
  • Sports: Drafting players or creating teams

Practice Problems

1

How many ways can you choose 2 books from a collection of 7 books?

This is a combination problem since order doesn't matter.

$^7C_2 = \frac{7!}{2!(7-2)!} = \frac{5040}{2 × 120} = \frac{5040}{240} = 21$

Alternative calculation: $^7C_2 = \frac{7 × 6}{2 × 1} = \frac{42}{2} = 21$

2

A class has 25 students. How many ways can the teacher choose 4 students for a special project?

Order doesn't matter for group selection.

$^{25}C_4 = \frac{25!}{4!(25-4)!} = \frac{25!}{24 × 21!}$

Calculation: $^{25}C_4 = \frac{25 × 24 × 23 × 22}{4 × 3 × 2 × 1} = \frac{303,600}{24} = 12,650$

3

In poker, a flush is 5 cards of the same suit. How many possible flushes are there in a 52-card deck?

There are 4 suits, each with 13 cards.

For each suit: $^{13}C_5$ ways to choose 5 cards

Total flushes: $4 × ^{13}C_5 = 4 × \frac{13!}{5!(13-5)!} = 4 × \frac{1287}{120 × 24} = 4 × 5148 = 5,148$

Wait, this counts royal flushes, etc. But the question asks for total flushes.

4

How many ways can you select 3 toppings from 6 available pizza toppings?

Order of topping selection doesn't matter.

$^6C_3 = \frac{6!}{3!(6-3)!} = \frac{720}{6 × 6} = \frac{720}{36} = 20$

Alternative: $^6C_3 = \frac{6 × 5 × 4}{3 × 2 × 1} = \frac{120}{6} = 20$

Key Takeaways

  • Combinations count selections where order doesn't matter
  • The formula $^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$ gives the number of ways to choose r items from n
  • Combinations are always smaller than permutations for the same n and r
  • $^nC_r = ^nC_{n-r}$ (symmetric property)
  • Common applications include lottery odds, committee selection, and sampling
  • The formula can be simplified to avoid large factorials: $^nC_r = \frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r(r-1)\cdots(1)}$
