Equation of a Line Perpendicular to a Vector \(\vec{n}\)
1.
The equation of a line passing through point A(\(x_1, y_1\)) and perpendicular to vector \(\vec{n} = (a, b)\) is: $$ a(x-x_1) + b(y-y_1) = 0 $$ The vector equation is \(\vec{n} \cdot (\vec{p} - \vec{a}) = 0\).
2.
The vector \(\vec{n} = (a, b)\) is perpendicular to the line \(ax + by + c = 0\). This vector \(\vec{n}\) is called a normal vector to the line.
1. Line through A(\(x_1, y_1\)) perpendicular to \(\vec{n}=(a,b)\)
Let A be a point on the line with position vector \(\vec{a}\), and P be any point on the line with position vector \(\vec{p}\). The vector \(\vec{AP} = \vec{p} - \vec{a}\) lies on the line. Given a vector \(\vec{n}\) that is perpendicular (normal) to the line. Since \(\vec{AP}\) is on the line and \(\vec{n}\) is perpendicular to the line, their dot product is zero: $$ \vec{n} \cdot \vec{AP} = 0 $$ $$ \vec{n} \cdot (\vec{p} - \vec{a}) = 0 $$ If A = (\(x_1, y_1\)), P = (\(x, y\)), and \(\vec{n} = (a, b)\), then \(\vec{p} - \vec{a} = (x - x_1, y - y_1)\). Substituting into the dot product: $$ (a,b) \cdot (x - x_1, y - y_1) = 0 $$ $$ a(x - x_1) + b(y - y_1) = 0 $$ This is the Cartesian equation of the line.

2. \(\vec{n}=(a,b)\) is normal to \(ax+by+c=0\)
Consider the line \(ax+by+c=0\). Let P(\(x_P, y_P\)) and Q(\(x_Q, y_Q\)) be two distinct points on this line. Then \(ax_P + by_P + c = 0\) and \(ax_Q + by_Q + c = 0\). Subtracting the second equation from the first gives: \(a(x_P - x_Q) + b(y_P - y_Q) = 0\). This can be written as a dot product: \((a,b) \cdot (x_P - x_Q, y_P - y_Q) = 0\). Let \(\vec{n} = (a,b)\). The vector \(\vec{QP} = (x_P - x_Q, y_P - y_Q)\) is a direction vector for the line. Since \(\vec{n} \cdot \vec{QP} = 0\), the vector \(\vec{n}=(a,b)\) is perpendicular to the line \(ax+by+c=0\).
ðŸ’¡ Normal Vector Insights:
  • The vector equation \(\vec{n} \cdot (\vec{p} - \vec{a}) = 0\) can be rewritten as \(\vec{n} \cdot \vec{p} = \vec{n} \cdot \vec{a}\). If we let \(k = \vec{n} \cdot \vec{a}\) (which is a scalar constant), the equation becomes \(\vec{n} \cdot \vec{p} = k\).
  • If \(\vec{n}=(a,b)\) and \(\vec{p}=(x,y)\), then \(\vec{n} \cdot \vec{p} = k\) becomes \(ax+by=k\). This is equivalent to the form \(ax+by+c=0\), where \(c = -k\).
  • Any non-zero scalar multiple of a normal vector is also a normal vector to the same line. For example, if \(\vec{n}=(a,b)\) is a normal vector, then so is \( (2a, 2b) \) or \( (-a, -b) \).
  • In 3D space, the equation of a plane is similarly defined: a point A on the plane, a normal vector \(\vec{n}=(a,b,c)\), and any point P(\(x,y,z\)) on the plane leads to \(a(x-x_1)+b(y-y_1)+c(z-z_1)=0\).
