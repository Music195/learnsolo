Distance between Origin O and Point P(\(a, b, c\))
The distance between the origin O(0,0,0) and a point P(\(a, b, c\)) in 3D space is given by: $$ OP = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2} $$
Derivation of the Distance Formula:
The distance of a point P(\(a,b,c\)) from the origin O(0,0,0) in a 3D Cartesian coordinate system is an extension of the Pythagorean theorem.

1. Let Q be the projection of point P onto the xy-plane. The coordinates of Q are (\(a,b,0\)).
2. The distance OQ in the xy-plane can be found using the 2D distance formula (which is derived from the Pythagorean theorem): $$ OQ^2 = a^2 + b^2 $$ So, \( OQ = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \).

3. Now, consider the right-angled triangle \(\triangle OQP\). The right angle is at Q.
  • The length of OQ is \(\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\).
  • The length of QP is the absolute difference in the z-coordinates, which is \(|c-0| = |c|\). So, \(QP^2 = c^2\).
  • OP is the hypotenuse.
4. By the Pythagorean theorem in \(\triangle OQP\): $$ OP^2 = OQ^2 + QP^2 $$ Substituting the values: $$ OP^2 = (a^2 + b^2) + c^2 $$ $$ OP^2 = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 $$ Therefore, the distance OP is: $$ OP = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2} $$ This formula gives the magnitude of the position vector \(\vec{OP} = (a,b,c)\).
ðŸ’¡ Key Insights:
  • This formula is a direct application of the Pythagorean theorem in three dimensions. You can think of it as applying the theorem twice: first in the xy-plane to find the projection, and then in the vertical plane containing that projection and the point P.
  • The distance \(OP\) is also the magnitude (or length) of the position vector \(\vec{OP} = \langle a, b, c \rangle\), often denoted as \(|\vec{OP}|\).
  • For a point P(\(x,y,z\)), the formula is \(OP = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}\). The use of \(a,b,c\) is just a common convention for specific coordinates.
  • In 2D, for a point P(\(a,b\)), the distance from the origin is \(OP = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\). The 3D formula is a natural extension.
  • This concept can be generalized to n-dimensional space. For a point \(P(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)\), the distance from the origin is \(\sqrt{x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \ldots + x_n^2}\).
