General Forms of Linear and Quadratic Functions

Mathematical Foundations

Functions can be classified by their degree - the highest power of the variable. Linear functions have degree 1, while quadratic functions have degree 2. This fundamental difference leads to distinct behaviors and applications.

Linear Functions (Degree 1)

Linear functions create straight lines when graphed. They have a constant rate of change and can model situations with steady growth or decline.

  • General form: $y = mx + b$
  • Slope $m$ determines steepness
  • y-intercept $b$ is where the line crosses the y-axis
  • Domain: all real numbers
  • Range: all real numbers

Quadratic Functions (Degree 2)

Quadratic functions create parabolas when graphed. They can model projectile motion, area problems, and optimization scenarios.

  • General form: $y = ax^2 + bx + c$
  • Coefficient $a$ determines direction and width
  • Coefficient $b$ affects horizontal shift
  • Constant $c$ is the y-intercept
  • Domain: all real numbers
  • Range: depends on direction of parabola

Worked Examples

Example 1: Linear Function

Consider $y = 2x + 3$

This is a linear function where:

  • Slope = 2 (steeper than $y = x$)
  • y-intercept = 3 (crosses y-axis at 3)
  • As x increases by 1, y increases by 2

Example 2: Quadratic Function

Consider $y = x^2 - 4x + 3$

This is a quadratic function where:

  • $a = 1$ (opens upward)
  • $b = -4$ (shifts the axis of symmetry)
  • $c = 3$ (y-intercept)
  • Can be factored as $y = (x-1)(x-3)$

Example 3: Comparing Functions

Linear: $y = 3x - 2$ vs Quadratic: $y = x^2 + 3x - 2$

Both have the same y-intercept (-2), but the quadratic has a squared term that creates curvature.

Key Takeaways

  • Linear functions have the form $y = mx + b$ and graph as straight lines
  • Quadratic functions have the form $y = ax^2 + bx + c$ and graph as parabolas
  • The coefficient $a$ in quadratic functions determines the direction and width of the parabola
  • Linear functions have constant slope, while quadratic functions have changing slope
  • Both types of functions have domains of all real numbers, but their ranges differ
  • Quadratic functions can model real-world phenomena that linear functions cannot
