Mathematical Foundation

Square roots are fundamental operations in mathematics, representing the inverse of squaring. The properties of square roots establish important relationships between squares, absolute values, and the behavior of square root functions. Understanding these properties is crucial for simplifying expressions, solving equations, and working with radicals in algebra.

Key Properties of Square Roots

Property 1: Squaring Square Roots
For any non-negative real number a: $(\sqrt{a})^2 = a$ and $(-\sqrt{a})^2 = a$. Both positive and negative square roots square to the same positive result.
Property 2: Square Root of a Square
$\sqrt{a^2} = |a|$ for all real numbers a. The square root of a squared number always gives the absolute value.
Property 3: Domain Restriction
Square roots are only defined for non-negative real numbers. $\sqrt{a}$ exists only when a â‰¥ 0.
Property 4: Principal Square Root
The principal square root (denoted $\sqrt{a}$) is always non-negative. The negative square root is denoted as $-\sqrt{a}$.

Worked Example 1: Squaring Square Roots

Evaluate $(\sqrt{9})^2$ and $(-\sqrt{9})^2$.

Solution:

$(\sqrt{9})^2 = 3^2 = 9$

$(-\sqrt{9})^2 = (-3)^2 = 9$

Both expressions equal 9, demonstrating Property 1.

Worked Example 2: Square Root of a Square

Evaluate $\sqrt{16}$, $\sqrt{(-4)^2}$, and $\sqrt{(-5)^2}$.

Solution:

$\sqrt{16} = 4$ (principal square root)

$\sqrt{(-4)^2} = \sqrt{16} = 4$

$\sqrt{(-5)^2} = \sqrt{25} = 5$

In all cases, we get the absolute value of the original number.
