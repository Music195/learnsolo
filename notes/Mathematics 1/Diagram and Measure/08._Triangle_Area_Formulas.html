Triangle Area Formulas

Mathematical Foundation

The area of a triangle can be calculated using various formulas depending on what information is known. The trigonometric area formula is particularly useful when you know two sides and the included angle, or when you need to relate area to trigonometric functions. This formula connects the area of a triangle to the sine of an angle, providing a powerful tool for solving geometric problems.

Trigonometric Area Formula

Area = (1/2) × a × b × sin C
Most general form - works for any triangle when you know two sides and the included angle

Base-Height Formula

Area = (1/2) × base × height
Traditional formula requiring perpendicular height from vertex to opposite side

Heron's Formula

Area = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)]
Uses only the three side lengths (s = semiperimeter)

Derivation: Trigonometric Area Formula

Consider △ABC with sides a, b, c and angle C at vertex C:

  • Drop a perpendicular from C to side AB (or its extension)
  • This creates height h from C to side AB
  • The area can be expressed as (1/2) × base × height

Case 1: Acute triangle

Area = (1/2) × a × h, where h = b × sin A

But sin A = sin(180° - C) = sin C

Therefore: Area = (1/2) × a × (b × sin C) = (1/2) × a × b × sin C

Case 2: Obtuse triangle

When angle C is obtuse, the height falls outside the triangle

Area = (1/2) × a × h, where h = b × sin(180° - C) = b × sin C

The formula still works because sin C remains positive

Height and Angle Relationship

The height h from vertex C to side AB satisfies:

  • h = a × sin B (height from C to AB)
  • h = b × sin A (same height from C to AB)
  • h = c × sin A × sin B / sin C (using Law of Sines)

This shows how the trigonometric area formula connects the area to the sine of the included angle.

Worked Example 1: SAS Method

Find the area of △ABC where a = 8 cm, b = 6 cm, and ∠C = 45°.

Solution:

Area = (1/2) × a × b × sin C

Area = (1/2) × 8 × 6 × sin 45°

Area = 24 × 0.7071 ≈ 16.97 cm²

Worked Example 2: Base-Height Method

Find the area of △ABC where base c = 10 cm and height h = 8 cm.

Solution:

Area = (1/2) × base × height

Area = (1/2) × 10 × 8 = 40 cm²

Worked Example 3: Heron's Formula

Find the area of △ABC where sides are 7 cm, 8 cm, and 9 cm.

Solution:

Semiperimeter s = (7 + 8 + 9)/2 = 12

Area = √[12(12-7)(12-8)(12-9)] = √[12×5×4×3] = √[720] ≈ 26.83 cm²

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Find the area of a triangle with sides 5 cm and 7 cm and included angle 60°.
Solution:
Area = (1/2) × 5 × 7 × sin 60° = (35/2) × 0.8660 ≈ 15.16 cm²
Problem 2: △ABC has sides a = 12, b = 9, c = 15. Find the area.
Solution:
First find angle C using Law of Cosines:
cos C = (a² + b² - c²)/(2ab) = (144 + 81 - 225)/(2×12×9) = 0/216 = 0
C = 90°
Area = (1/2) × a × b × sin C = (1/2) × 12 × 9 × sin 90° = 54 × 1 = 54
Problem 3: The area of △ABC is 24 cm² and ∠C = 30°. If a = 8 cm, find b.
Solution:
Area = (1/2) × a × b × sin C
24 = (1/2) × 8 × b × sin 30°
24 = 4 × b × 0.5
24 = 2b
b = 12 cm

Key Takeaways

  • Trigonometric: Area = (1/2)ab sin C (most versatile)
  • Base-Height: Area = (1/2)base × height (requires perpendicular height)
  • Heron's Formula: Area = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)] (uses only sides)
  • Maximum Area: For fixed perimeter, equilateral triangle has maximum area
  • Angle Relationship: Area depends on sine of included angle
  • Units: Area units are square of length units
  • Triangle Inequality: Must be satisfied for valid triangle
  • Right Triangle: Reduces to (1/2)ab when C = 90°
  • Acute/Obtuse: All formulas work for any triangle type
  • Applications: Surveying, physics, computer graphics, engineering
